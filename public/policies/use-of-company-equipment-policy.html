1. About This Policy

This policy governs company assets provided to employees of Unhinged Development Group Ltd (trading as Groomr) — primarily laptops and peripherals, but also software licences, system access, and credentials. It sets out acceptable use, security obligations, what happens when equipment is lost, and the process for returning assets and revoking access when employment ends.

This policy is particularly important at Groomr because employees with access to company systems also have access to Supabase (user and booking data), Stripe (financial data), Clerk (authentication), and Cloudinary (media assets). A lost, compromised, or unencrypted device is a mandatory ICO breach notification. The security requirements in this policy are not optional.

This policy applies to all employees. Monitoring of company devices is disclosed here and in the Employee Privacy Notice. It is governed by Scots law and falls within the jurisdiction of Employment Tribunals Scotland.

2. What Is Covered

This policy covers all assets provided by the company for work purposes, including:

  • Physical hardware: Laptops, monitors, keyboards, mice, headsets, and any other peripherals issued to you.
  • Software licences: Any applications installed on company hardware or provided as SaaS under company subscription.
  • System credentials and access: Login details, API keys, SSH keys, and access permissions for company systems — including GitHub, Supabase, Stripe, Cloudinary, Clerk, Google Workspace, and Slack. Access credentials are company assets even though they are not physical objects.

The company provides a laptop to all employees from day one. There is no bring-your-own-device (BYOD) arrangement in place. Work must be done on company-provided hardware.

3. Acceptable Use

Company equipment is provided for work purposes. Reasonable incidental personal use — checking personal email, brief personal browsing — is permitted during breaks and outside working hours, provided it does not interfere with work, does not expose company systems to security risk, and does not involve accessing illegal content.

The following are prohibited on company equipment at all times:

  • Accessing, downloading, or storing illegal content of any kind.
  • Saving work files to personal cloud storage (personal iCloud, Google Drive, Dropbox, or equivalent).
  • Installing software that could introduce malware or security vulnerabilities without approval.
  • Using company credentials on personal devices or personal accounts.
  • Allowing third parties — including family members — to use company equipment or access company accounts.
  • Disabling or circumventing security controls (encryption, screen lock, MFA).

Deliberate misuse of company equipment is gross misconduct and may result in summary dismissal.

4. Security Requirements

The following security controls are mandatory on all company equipment. These are minimum standards under UK GDPR Article 32 — they are not optional and are not subject to personal preference.

  • Full-disk encryption: FileVault must be enabled on macOS devices; BitLocker on Windows devices. Confirm this is active when you first receive your device and after any OS reinstall.
  • Screen lock: Set your device to lock automatically after a maximum of 10 minutes idle. Lock your screen manually whenever you step away.
  • Multi-factor authentication (MFA): Must be enabled on all work accounts — Google Workspace, GitHub, Supabase, Stripe, Clerk, Cloudinary, and any other service with access to company or user data. No exceptions.
  • VPN on public Wi-Fi: Connect via a VPN before conducting any work on public or untrusted networks (cafés, hotels, airports).
  • No personal cloud storage: Work files must not be saved to personal cloud accounts. Use company-approved storage only.
  • Software updates: Keep your operating system and applications up to date. Do not defer security updates.

4.1 Monitoring

The company uses Google Workspace administrative tools, which provide usage logs and visibility over company accounts and devices. This monitoring is disclosed here and in the Employee Privacy Notice. No covert monitoring tools are in use. If this changes, employees will be informed and the Privacy Notice updated before any new monitoring begins.

Data breach risk: If a device is lost or stolen and you cannot confirm it was encrypted, the company must self-report to the ICO within 72 hours. This window begins from the moment the loss is known. Report lost or stolen devices to the Founder immediately — do not wait to see if the device turns up.

5. Software & Services

You may install software on your company laptop that is required for your role and does not introduce a security risk. There is no formal software whitelist at this stage — use reasonable judgement, prioritise well-known and maintained tools, and do not install software from untrusted sources.

If you are unsure whether a particular tool is appropriate to install, ask the Founder before installing it. This is especially important for tools that handle data, network traffic, or system permissions.

Open-source contribution using company equipment is permitted for public, non-competing projects. Any contribution that touches Groomr platform code, architecture, or data — or that could be construed as disclosing proprietary information — requires Founder approval before publication. Inadvertently open-sourcing proprietary code is an irreversible event.

Software licences provided by the company are for business use only. Do not use company-licensed software for personal projects or share licences with people outside the company.

6. Asset Register & Reporting Loss

All company hardware is recorded on an asset register at the time of issue, including serial numbers and the employee it is assigned to. You will be asked to confirm receipt of equipment when it is first issued to you.

If company equipment is lost, stolen, or significantly damaged, you must:

  • Notify the Founder immediately — not once you have assessed the situation, but as soon as you become aware of the loss.
  • Report the theft to the police if applicable, and retain the crime reference number.
  • Provide the Founder with enough information to assess whether remote wipe is necessary.

The company will determine whether remote wipe is required based on the nature of the loss and whether the device was encrypted. Remote wipe capability is maintained through Google Workspace device management.

Accidental damage in the course of normal use is not treated as a disciplinary matter. Damage caused by recklessness or deliberate misuse is.

7. Return on Termination

All company equipment must be returned to the company on your last day of employment, or by a date agreed in writing with the Founder. This includes all hardware, accessories, and any physical materials (printed documents, cables, accessories) issued to you.

Before returning a device, do not attempt to wipe or factory-reset it yourself. The company will manage the return and any necessary data removal. Remove personal files from the device and transfer them to personal storage before your last day.

Failure to return company equipment may result in a deduction from your final pay. Note: a wage deduction for unreturned equipment is only lawful where your employment contract contains an express written clause authorising it — this is governed by your contract, not by this policy alone. Ensure you review your contract for this provision.

Return logistics (courier, in-person handover) will be agreed between you and the Founder as part of the offboarding process.

8. Access Revocation on Exit

On your last day of employment, all system access will be revoked. This is handled by the Founder, who is responsible for the access revocation checklist. Revocation covers all company systems and accounts.

Access to be revoked includes, but is not limited to:

  • Google Workspace (email, Drive, Calendar, Meet)
  • GitHub — repositories and organisation membership
  • Supabase — database access and project membership
  • Stripe — dashboard and API key access
  • Clerk — dashboard access
  • Cloudinary — media account access
  • Slack — workspace membership
  • Any other service or tool with access to company or user data

Important: A departing employee who retains access to live production systems is both a data protection risk and a potential Computer Misuse Act 1990 exposure once employment has ended and access is no longer authorised. Revocation is therefore treated as a same-day priority, not an administrative afterthought.

If you discover after leaving that you still have access to any company system, notify the Founder immediately so that it can be closed. Continued access to company systems after employment ends without authorisation is a criminal offence under the Computer Misuse Act 1990.
