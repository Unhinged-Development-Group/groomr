1. About This Policy

This policy sets out how sickness absence is managed at Unhinged Development Group Ltd (trading as Groomr). It covers how to report absence, Statutory Sick Pay, Company Sick Pay, return-to-work procedures, and what happens during long-term absence.

The policy applies to all employees. It does not apply to independent groomers or contractors on the Groomr platform.

Sickness records are special-category health data under UK GDPR and the Data Protection Act 2018. They are processed on the basis of employment law obligation and handled in accordance with the Employee Privacy Notice.

This policy is governed by Scots law. Employment claims arising under it fall within the jurisdiction of Employment Tribunals Scotland.

2. Reporting Sickness

If you are unable to work due to illness or injury, you must contact your line manager by telephone — not by text or email — as early as possible on the first day of absence, and before your normal start time where possible.

Each subsequent day of absence, or by arrangement at the start of a longer absence, you should keep your manager updated on your expected return date.

If you are absent for more than 7 consecutive calendar days, you must obtain a fit note from your GP and submit it to the company without delay. A fit note is required to maintain eligibility for Statutory Sick Pay from the eighth day of absence.

3. Self-Certification & Fit Notes

For absences of up to and including 7 calendar days, you can self-certify your illness. No medical evidence is required for the first week.

From the eighth calendar day, a fit note from your GP or treating clinician is required. Fit notes may certify that you are not fit for work, or that you may be fit for work with adjustments. Where a fit note suggests adjustments — such as reduced hours, amended duties, or phased return — the company will consider these positively and implement them where operationally practicable.

You must submit all fit notes promptly. If a new fit note is needed to cover a continuing absence, it is your responsibility to obtain it before the existing one expires.

4. Statutory Sick Pay

Statutory Sick Pay (SSP) is a legal entitlement. Eligible employees receive SSP from the fourth qualifying day of absence (the first three qualifying days are "waiting days" for which SSP is not payable).

Key SSP facts:

  • Current rate: £116.75 per week (April 2025 rate — verify current rate).
  • Maximum duration: Up to 28 weeks in any period of incapacity for work.
  • Eligibility: You must be earning above the Lower Earnings Limit and meet the qualifying conditions set by HMRC.

SSP is paid through payroll in the same way as your salary. Where Company Sick Pay applies, it is paid in addition to (or in lieu of) SSP — see section 5.

5. Company Sick Pay

In addition to SSP, the company provides Company Sick Pay (CSP) to employees who have successfully completed their probationary period.

CSP entitlement (post-probation):

6 weeks at full pay, followed by 6 weeks at half pay

Qualifying condition: Must have passed probation

Period: Per rolling 12-month period

CSP is paid through payroll and is inclusive of any SSP entitlement — you will not receive both separately. During the full-pay period, your gross pay is maintained at your normal rate. During the half-pay period, you receive half your normal gross pay plus any SSP you are eligible for up to your normal pay level (ensuring you do not receive more than normal pay in total).

Employees who have not yet passed probation receive SSP only, in accordance with the statutory rules.

CSP is a discretionary enhancement above the statutory floor. The company reserves the right to withhold or recover CSP where it has reasonable grounds to believe the absence was not genuine — in which case the matter will be managed through the Disciplinary Procedure.

6. Return to Work

On returning from any period of sickness absence, your line manager will conduct a brief return-to-work conversation. This is a supportive check-in, not a formal meeting, and covers:

  • How you are feeling and whether you are well enough to return.
  • Whether any adjustments to your duties or hours would help your return.
  • Any work that needs to be handed back or prioritised.

Return-to-work conversations are recorded briefly and held on file. They are not disciplinary in nature. A pattern of short-term absences may, over time, lead to a more formal absence management conversation — but a single return-to-work check-in is routine and should not cause concern.

Where a fit note has suggested a phased return or amended duties, these will be discussed and agreed at the return-to-work conversation.

7. Long-Term Absence

Where absence extends beyond four weeks or appears likely to do so, the company will manage the situation more actively. This is not a punitive process — the goal is to understand your situation and find a way to support your recovery and return.

The company will handle long-term absence informally at this stage, rather than through a formal occupational health referral pathway. This means the Founder or line manager will maintain regular contact with you (typically every two to four weeks), check your wellbeing, and explore whether any adjustments, additional support, or a phased return timeline is appropriate.

If the company needs to understand your medical position in more detail, it may ask your GP or treating specialist to provide a report, subject to your consent under the Access to Medical Reports Act 1988. You have the right to see and comment on any such report before it is submitted.

Where a long-term absence cannot be resolved through adjustments or phased return, and a return to the role becomes unlikely, the company will follow a fair capability process before taking any further action. This process will always include medical evidence, proper consultation, and the opportunity to appeal.

8. Disability & Reasonable Adjustments

Under the Equality Act 2010, a person is disabled if they have a physical or mental impairment that has a substantial and long-term adverse effect on their ability to carry out normal day-to-day activities. Where this threshold is met, the company has a duty to make reasonable adjustments.

The duty to make reasonable adjustments applies to physical workplace features, provisions, criteria and practices, and the provision of auxiliary aids. If you have a health condition that may amount to a disability, we encourage you to tell us so that we can consider what adjustments might help.

Important: The company will not take any capability action in relation to disability-related absence without first considering whether reasonable adjustments could enable the employee to remain in work. Dismissal without exploring adjustments would constitute disability discrimination. Legal advice will always be obtained before any such action is considered.

Reasonable adjustments are considered on a case-by-case basis. There is no exhaustive list of what is reasonable — it depends on the nature of the condition, the role, and the operational context.

9. Mental Health Support

Mental health conditions are treated in exactly the same way as physical health conditions under this policy. There is no distinction in how absences are recorded, managed, or supported.

If you are struggling with your mental health — whether work-related or personal — please speak to your line manager or the Founder. Alternatively, the Employee Assistance Programme (EAP) provides free, confidential counselling and support, available 24 hours a day. You do not need to tell the company you are using it.

The company takes work-related stress seriously as a health and safety matter. If your workload, role, or working conditions are contributing to poor mental health, raise this as early as possible so that we can address it. The goal is always to resolve the underlying issue, not to manage the symptom.
