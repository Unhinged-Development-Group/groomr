# Code of Conduct — Contents Breakdown

**Scope: Behavioural standards for all Groomr users — owners, groomers, and team members**

---

## 1. Purpose & scope

- Governs how all users are expected to behave **on and through the Groomr platform** — in messaging, listings, bookings, reviews, and any other platform interaction.
- Applies to **both owners and groomers** (and groomer team members, who act under their salon owner's account). Some sections address only one side — this should be clearly marked within the document.
- Covers conduct that occurs **via Groomr's infrastructure** (messaging, reviews, booking notes, profile content). It does not directly govern what happens in the grooming salon itself, but it does set expectations that feed into the Grooming Standards and Disputes policies.
- Sits beneath the Terms of Service (ToS), which is the primary binding contract. The Code of Conduct gives the ToS teeth — it operationalises the acceptable use obligations already in the ToS and explains what enforcement looks like in practice.
- Cross-references: Disputes Policy (what happens when conduct causes a service failure), Privacy Policy (personal data shared in messages), Grooming Standards Policy (animal-welfare conduct during the service).

## 2. Regulatory drivers

| Law | What it obliges | Tag |
|---|---|---|
| **Online Safety Act 2023** | Groomr is a user-to-user service (messaging, reviews, profiles) and carries **Category 2A duties** unless below the threshold for categorisation. Regardless of threshold, Groomr must have clear and accessible terms setting out what user-generated content/conduct is prohibited and how it enforces them. A Code of Conduct is the primary mechanism for meeting this. Illegal content (harassment, animal cruelty, fraud, CSAM) must be actioned promptly on notice. | [REQUIRED] |
| **Electronic Commerce (EC Directive) Regs 2002** | Hosting safe harbour (reg 19) applies only if Groomr acts expeditiously on notice of unlawful material. A published conduct policy + enforcement process is how "notice" is formally received and acted on. Without a clear policy, the "we didn't know" defence weakens. | [REQUIRED] |
| **Consumer Protection from Unfair Trading Regs 2008** | Prohibits misleading acts and omissions — fake reviews, inflated qualifications, false pricing claims in listings. Groomr must not facilitate these practices (facilitator liability risk if it turns a blind eye). | [REQUIRED] |
| **Equality Act 2010** | Groomers and owners both have protected characteristics. Refusal of service or discriminatory messaging based on those characteristics (race, disability, religion, etc.) can give rise to claims. The Code of Conduct must prohibit discriminatory conduct explicitly — both groomer-on-owner and owner-on-groomer. | [REQUIRED] |
| **Animal Health & Welfare (Scotland) Act 2006 / Animal Welfare Act 2006 (E&W) / Welfare of Animals Act (NI) 2011** | Groomers have a statutory duty of care to animals in their care. Conduct provisions relating to how animals must be treated — and the obligation to disclose/flag welfare concerns — attach to the applicable Act for the location of the groom. | [REQUIRED] — applicable Act follows location of service |
| **UK GDPR / Data Protection Act 2018** | Users sharing personal data about third parties in messages or booking notes (e.g. an owner sharing a neighbour's dog details) must not be facilitated in ways that breach data-protection obligations. The Code of Conduct should prohibit collection of other users' personal data outside the platform's intended use. | [REQUIRED] |
| **Consumer Rights Act 2015** | Groomers' listings and service descriptions must accurately represent what they offer. Misleading descriptions are not just a trading-regs issue — they can be an unfair contract term. The conduct policy should prohibit inaccurate listings and connect to the remedy pathway. | [BEST PRACTICE] to cite explicitly; underpins the broader prohibition |
| **Malicious Communications Act 1988 / Communications Act 2003, s.127** | Sending grossly offensive, threatening, or false messages via Groomr's messaging system is a criminal offence. The policy should state this plainly so users understand the consequences extend beyond deactivation. | [BEST PRACTICE] — plain-language signpost; not a direct obligation on Groomr |
| **Prescription and Limitation (Scotland) Act 1973** | Sets the outer window for claims arising from conduct (5-year short negative prescription for contractual/delictual claims under Scots law). However, conduct that constitutes a civil wrong against an English or Welsh user will be actionable in that user's local court — the policy cannot require Scots jurisdiction for disputes between individual users over conduct matters.

## 3. Recommended sections

### 3.1 Who this applies to
- All registered users: dog owners, independent groomers, groomer team members.
- Groomer team members' conduct is attributed to the salon-owner account — the groomer account holder is responsible for their team.
- Applies from account creation and continues after any account closure for conduct that occurred while active.
- **[REQUIRED]** — scope ambiguity is an enforcement loophole.

### 3.2 Conduct in messaging
- Use Groomr's messaging system for its intended purpose: discussing bookings, care requirements, and service arrangements.
- Prohibited: harassment, threats, intimidating language, unsolicited promotional content, attempting to move the booking off-platform (to avoid the platform fee — this is a commercial issue and a fraud risk).
- Required disclosure: if a groomer cannot fulfil a booking, they must communicate through the platform, not disappear.
- **[REQUIRED]** — messaging is a user-to-user service directly in scope for OSA 2023 and the intermediary defence.

### 3.3 Profile and listing accuracy (groomers)
- All information in a groomer profile must be accurate and kept up to date: qualifications, insurance, location, services offered, pricing.
- Prohibited: claiming qualifications not held, claiming City & Guilds / iPET Network / other accreditation without evidence, using misleading before/after photos (e.g. photos of another groomer's work).
- Insurance must be in force at the time of any booking — lapsed insurance must be disclosed and the listing updated or taken down.
- **[REQUIRED]** — false listings create Groomr liability under the consumer protection framework and expose the intermediary safe harbour.

### 3.4 Review integrity
- Reviews must reflect the reviewer's genuine experience of the service in question.
- Prohibited for owners: submitting a review for a service not received, incentivised reviews, reviewing on behalf of someone else, review-bombing (coordinated negative reviews).
- Prohibited for groomers: soliciting fake positive reviews, offering discounts/gifts conditional on a positive review, pressuring owners not to leave negative reviews, posting false retaliatory replies.
- Groomr must not manipulate ranking or review visibility in ways that create a false impression — cite Consumer Protection from Unfair Trading Regs 2008, reg 6 (misleading omissions).
- **[REQUIRED]** — fake reviews are expressly covered by the Online Safety Act 2023 (online fraud) and the consumer trading regulations.

### 3.5 Animal welfare and disclosure obligations (groomers)
- Groomers must not perform a groom on an animal showing signs of extreme distress, acute illness, or injury — they have a duty to pause and contact the owner.
- Groomers must disclose any incident during a groom (injury, adverse reaction, distress) to the owner promptly and through the platform.
- If a groomer believes an animal in their care is suffering (beyond normal grooming stress), they have a welfare obligation under the applicable Act — the policy should direct them to follow that obligation even if it delays or cancels the appointment.
- Owners must accurately disclose known aggression, health conditions, and behavioural flags at the time of booking. Failure to disclose is a breach of conduct, not just a service issue.
- A groomer may refuse service on animal-welfare grounds without this constituting a breach of the platform rules.
- **[REQUIRED]** — welfare obligations are statutory; conduct policy is how Groomr documents compliance with its duty not to facilitate breach.

### 3.6 Non-discrimination
- Neither party may refuse service, provide inferior service, or conduct themselves differently based on: age, disability, gender reassignment, marriage/civil partnership, pregnancy/maternity, race, religion/belief, sex, or sexual orientation (protected characteristics under the Equality Act 2010).
- Groomers may decline bookings for legitimate operational reasons (too far outside travel radius, dog size outside equipment capacity, no slots available). The policy must distinguish legitimate refusal from discriminatory refusal.
- Owners must not harass groomers based on any protected characteristic.
- Groomr's enforcement process must itself be applied consistently — biased or discriminatory enforcement decisions expose Groomr to direct liability under the Act.
- **[REQUIRED]**

### 3.7 Off-platform payment prohibition
- Groomrs facilitates booking and payment. Deliberately directing a booking off-platform to avoid the commission fee is prohibited.
- This includes: sharing bank details or alternative payment links before or during a booking, arranging future repeat appointments verbally outside the platform after an initial Groomr booking, or instructing an owner to pay cash and not record it.
- **[COMMERCIAL CHOICE]** on how strictly to enforce this and whether to include a grace period for cash top-ups (e.g. owner tips an extra £5 in cash) — but the prohibition on deliberate fee avoidance is [REQUIRED] for the platform to be commercially viable.

### 3.8 Prohibited conduct — exhaustive list
This section is the hard no-list. Include:
- Impersonation of another user, groomer, or Groomr staff.
- Using the platform to advertise services outside of Groomr (lead generation for other booking channels).
- Attempting to access another user's account or data.
- Uploading content that is illegal, obscene, threatening, or relates to animal cruelty.
- Fraudulent booking claims (e.g. booking a slot and not attending to block a competitor's availability).
- Sharing other users' personal data outside the platform without consent.
- Any conduct that Groomr reasonably determines undermines trust and safety on the platform.
- **[REQUIRED]** — the catch-all clause is important for OSA 2023 compliance and the intermediary safe-harbour position.

### 3.9 Reporting and how Groomr handles reports
- Explain how users report conduct breaches: in-app reporting (if built), email to a specified address, or via the dispute pathway for service-related conduct.
- Groomr will acknowledge reports within X working days and resolve within Y working days. (These timelines are a commercial decision — but stating them is [REQUIRED] under OSA 2023 for user-to-user services.)
- Groomr will not share the identity of the person who made a report without their consent, except where required by law.
- Outcomes are at Groomr's discretion within the published enforcement framework (see §3.10).
- **[REQUIRED]**

### 3.10 Enforcement — graduated consequences
State clearly what consequences can follow a breach. The gradient matters — instant permanent bans without a warning framework can be challenged as disproportionate under unfair-terms principles.
- Warning (first low-level breach).
- Content removal without account action.
- Temporary suspension of booking/messaging functionality.
- Account suspension pending investigation.
- Permanent deactivation and ban.
- Referral to law enforcement or animal welfare authorities (for criminal conduct or animal welfare offences).
Groomr should reserve the right to skip steps for serious conduct (fraud, animal cruelty, harassment). State that this discretion exists.
- **[REQUIRED]** — unfair-terms challenge risk if enforcement is arbitrary; OSA 2023 requires accessible and transparent enforcement process.

### 3.11 Appeals
- Users must have a mechanism to appeal enforcement decisions. State the process, who handles it, and the timescale.
- Note that some decisions (permanent bans for criminal conduct referrals) may not be reversible pending the outcome of any law-enforcement process.
- ADR signpost: if a user believes Groomr's conduct enforcement has itself caused them a loss, direct them to the complaints procedure and the ADR scheme referenced in the ToS.
- **[REQUIRED]** — ADR signposting is a statutory obligation; appeals process is best practice but essential for proportionality under unfair-terms rules.

### 3.12 Groomr's own conduct standards
An often-omitted section but important for trust. Groomr commits to:
- Apply the Code consistently and without bias.
- Not use access to user data (messages, booking history) for purposes outside its Privacy Policy.
- Not manipulate search ranking or review visibility to favour commercial arrangements.
- Communicate enforcement decisions in plain language.
- **[BEST PRACTICE]** — builds user trust and supports the consumer fairness obligations. Platform-to-business ranking transparency is also relevant here (see §4).

## 4. Marketplace-specific considerations

**Intermediary posture.** Groomr's safest legal position is as a neutral facilitator. The more the Code of Conduct reads as Groomr adjudicating the quality of the grooming service, the more it looks like Groomr is taking responsibility for that service. Keep the conduct policy focused on *platform behaviour* (messaging, listings, reviews, payments), not on judging whether a groom was good enough — that's the Disputes Policy's job.

**Two-sided obligations.** Owners are consumers under the Consumer Rights Act and the CC Regs; groomers are business users. The Code must be readable by both, and the same conduct rule may have different legal weight depending on which side it applies to. Discriminatory refusal by a groomer is an Equality Act issue; discriminatory treatment of a groomer by an owner is also an Equality Act issue but the cause of action may differ.

**Stripe Connect.** Off-platform payment prohibition (§3.7) must be written knowing that groomers have their own Stripe Connect accounts — the prohibition is on *circumventing the booking*, not on the groomer accepting cash tips. Don't inadvertently prohibit all cash contact between owner and groomer.

**Team members as actors.** The `team_members` table and `assigned_to_team_member_id` in `appointments` mean that a team member can interact with a dog owner on behalf of the groomer account. The Code must be explicit that team members' conduct is attributed to the salon-owner account holder — the owner of the account carries the compliance risk for their staff.

**Review manipulation and rankings.** Groomr's search uses `average_rating` and `total_reviews` on `groomer_profiles`. Any internal manipulation of these fields (manually bumping a groomer's rating, suppressing legitimate negative reviews) would be a misleading commercial practice under the 2008 Regs. The conduct policy should apply to Groomr as much as to users on this point.

**Platform-to-business fairness (P2B Reg 2019/1150).** The UK status of this EU regulation post-Brexit is uncertain — verify current applicability. As a practical matter, transparency about ranking criteria, enforcement decisions, and appeals is best practice regardless of whether the Regulation formally applies.

## 5. Animal-welfare considerations

This section is directly load-bearing for the Code of Conduct, not just a cross-reference.

- **Groomer obligations during the groom:** duty to pause on signs of distress, duty to disclose incidents, obligation to prioritise welfare over completing a paid service (§3.5 above).
- **Owner obligations at the time of booking:** disclosure of aggression, health conditions (e.g. epilepsy, heart conditions, arthritis), vaccination status, and any prior incidents during grooming. Deliberate non-disclosure to secure a booking is a conduct breach, not just a civil matter.
- **Muzzling:** a groomer may require muzzling as a welfare/safety measure without this being discriminatory conduct. This should be stated explicitly to prevent owners from raising it as a complaint.
- **Refusal of service on welfare grounds:** a groomer who declines a booking because the dog is in poor health, shows extreme distress, or cannot be safely managed is acting in compliance with their statutory duty — the conduct policy must not punish this. Groomr should not penalise groomers for welfare-led refusals (e.g. do not count them negatively toward acceptance rate metrics if those are introduced).
- **Incident reporting to Groomr:** consider whether the policy requires groomers to notify Groomr (not just the owner) of welfare incidents during grooms. This would give Groomr early notice of claims, support the `disputes` and audit trail, and align with the OSA duty to act on notice of harm.

## 6. Cross-references

| Policy | How it connects |
|---|---|
| **Terms of Service** | Code of Conduct is a schedule or exhibit to the ToS; breaches of the Code are contractual breaches. The ToS must reference the Code explicitly. |
| **Disputes Policy** | When a conduct breach causes a service failure (e.g. groomer no-show, owner non-disclosure causing injury), the Disputes Policy governs resolution; the Code of Conduct governs whether the offending party faces enforcement action. These are parallel processes. |
| **Grooming Standards Policy** | The animal-welfare conduct obligations in the Code (§3.5) should map directly to the professional standards set there — avoid contradictions in what each document says a groomer must do. |
| **Privacy Policy** | Messaging content and conduct records are personal data. The Code must not create data-retention obligations that conflict with the Privacy Policy's stated retention periods. |
| **Acceptable Use Policy** | If there is a separate Acceptable Use Policy (covering uploaded content — photos, documents), the Code should cross-reference rather than duplicate the prohibited-content rules. |
| **Refunds & Payments Policy** | Off-platform payment prohibition (§3.7) interacts with the refund framework. If a booking is paid off-platform and goes wrong, Groomr has no payment record to action a refund against. The policies must say the same thing. |

## 7. Risk flags — get a solicitor on these

**OSA 2023 categorisation.** Whether Groomr falls into a regulated category under the Online Safety Act affects the specific duties it owes. At launch (Glasgow/Edinburgh, pre-revenue) it may be below threshold, but this should be assessed by a solicitor before launch, not assumed. The Code of Conduct must still meet the minimum accessibility and transparency standard regardless.

**Enforcement discretion vs. unfair terms.** Broadly-drafted enforcement clauses (e.g. "we can remove your account for any reason") are at risk of challenge under the CRA 2015 unfair-terms test (significant imbalance to the consumer's detriment). The graduated enforcement framework (§3.10) mitigates this, but the drafting needs solicitor review — particularly for groomer account deactivation, which could constitute termination of a commercial relationship with financial consequences.

**Equality Act exposure in enforcement.** If Groomr's enforcement decisions disproportionately affect users with a protected characteristic (e.g. if the automated flagging of messages disproportionately targets non-native English speakers), Groomr could face an indirect-discrimination claim. Enforcement policies should be reviewed for disparate-impact risk.

**Groomer worker/employment classification.** Any language in the Code that sounds like Groomr is *requiring* certain professional standards as a condition of using the platform (as opposed to describing minimum acceptable conduct) nudges toward a worker relationship. Solicitor review of the line between "platform rules" and "employer direction" is needed — particularly for anything about how groomers must conduct themselves during the groom itself.

**Animal welfare incident liability.** If a groomer follows the Code of Conduct obligations (discloses an incident, pauses the groom, contacts the owner) and the dog is still injured, could an owner argue Groomr's conduct policy created a duty of care? This is a novel area — get a view from a solicitor with Scots private-law experience before publishing the welfare-incident obligations.

## 8. Decisions needed from you

1. **Enforcement timelines (§3.9).** What are the acknowledgement and resolution SLAs for conduct reports? These will be in the published policy and create user expectations. If you can't operationally meet a 2-day acknowledgement, don't promise it.

2. **Report mechanism.** Is this an email inbox, an in-app report button, or both? The policy must name it. The in-app reporting button does not currently exist in the product — is that being built before launch, or are you starting with email?

3. **Off-platform payment grace cases (§3.7).** Where is the line? Cash tips during a groom? A standing arrangement with a repeat client who also uses Groomr for new clients? State clearly what's tolerated so the prohibition is enforceable.

4. **Welfare incident notification to Groomr (§5).** Do you want groomers to notify Groomr of welfare incidents in addition to the owner, or only the owner? Requiring notification to Groomr builds an audit trail but also makes you a more active participant in welfare events — weigh the liability against the trust signal.

5. **Appeals body.** Who handles appeals inside Groomr? At this stage it may just be the founder, but the policy needs to name a role or team — "a member of the Groomr team" is sufficient for now, but you should think about what happens as the team scales.

6. **Cross-border conduct.** If a groomer in Edinburgh treats an owner from Newcastle in a discriminatory way, both Scots and English/Welsh Equality Act routes could be relevant. The policy doesn't need to resolve the jurisdictional question, but you should have a view on which ADR scheme you're signposting.

7. **Grooming-standards policy.** Does this exist yet? The Code of Conduct (§3.5) will reference it. If not, it needs to be on the policy roadmap — the conduct document is weaker without it.
