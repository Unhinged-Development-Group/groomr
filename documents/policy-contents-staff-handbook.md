> Development Group Ltd (trading as Groomr). These are the policies that govern
> the employment relationship — entirely separate from the platform-facing policies
> (Terms of Service, Disputes, Refunds) that govern the owner↔groomer marketplace.
>
> Employment law in Great Britain is reserved to Westminster, so these policies
> apply on the same legal footing to Scottish and English/Welsh employees alike.
> The material Scotland-specific differences are procedural: Employment Tribunals
> Scotland handles claims from Scottish employees, Scots contract law governs the
> employment contracts (no consideration doctrine, different remedy rules), and debt
> enforcement is by diligence (arrestment, sheriff officers) not English bailiff/CCJ.

## What else would you need? — The full staff policy suite

The eight policies you listed are a good core, but on their own they leave several
legally required gaps and a few commercially important holes. Below is the complete
picture. Policies marked [REQUIRED] must exist before you have a first employee;
the rest are time-sensitive once you grow.

### Legally required from day one (or close to it)

| Policy | Why it's required |
|---|---|
| **Disciplinary & Grievance Policy** | ACAS Code of Practice (statutory). An Employment Tribunal *will* adjust any award upward (typically 25%) if you lack one or don't follow it. Must be in writing. |
| **Equal Opportunities & Anti-Discrimination Policy** | Equality Act 2010. Reduces tribunal exposure; required in practice even if not in statute as a standalone document. |
| **Anti-Harassment & Bullying Policy** | Equality Act 2010 (employer liability for harassment); also the new Worker Protection (Amendment of Equality Act 2010) Act 2023 from October 2024 — you have a positive duty to *prevent* sexual harassment. A policy is your primary evidence of that. |
| **Health & Safety Policy** | Health and Safety at Work Act 1974: written policy **required once you have 5 or more employees**. At sub-5 it's still best practice and you're still liable for breaches of the Act. |
| **Whistleblowing (Protected Disclosures) Policy** | Public Interest Disclosure Act 1998. Workers who make a qualifying disclosure are protected from detriment. A policy doesn't exempt you from the law but without one you'll mishandle a disclosure and face a tribunal. |
| **Employee Privacy Notice** | UK GDPR + DPA 2018. You must inform employees of the lawful basis for processing their data, what you hold, how long you keep it, and their rights. Without it, every payroll run is a potential breach. |
| **Parental Leave Policy** | Consolidates statutory rights (maternity, paternity, adoption, shared parental leave, parental bereavement leave). The rights exist in statute regardless — the policy documents what you pay above statutory minimum and how requests are handled. |
| **Flexible Working Policy** | Employment Relations (Flexible Working) Act 2023 (in force since April 2024): employees have the right to request flexible working **from day one** of employment, up to twice a year. You must deal with requests within two months. A policy is the only way to handle this consistently. |

### High priority — within first year or first hire

| Policy | Why |
|---|---|
| **Probation Policy** | Defines the probation period, review process, and extension/termination procedure. Without it, probationary dismissals are inconsistent and legally messier. |
| **Performance Management Policy** | Sets out how you manage underperformance — PIPs, timelines, support. The prerequisite to any capability-based dismissal going cleanly. |
| **Redundancy Policy** | Collective consultation rules apply at 20+ redundancies; statutory individual rights apply from day one. A policy means you follow a fair process and protect against unfair-dismissal claims. |
| **Social Media & Communications Policy** | Governs use of personal and company social media. Groomr's brand is built on trust — a groomer employee saying something damaging on LinkedIn while "working at Groomr" is a real risk. Covers confidentiality, monitoring, and conduct standards. |
| **Confidentiality & IP Assignment Policy** | Any employee writing code, designing features, or handling user data is generating IP. Without an assignment clause (in the contract) and a supporting policy, ownership of that IP is genuinely uncertain. Start-ups get burned by this post-investment. |

### Nice to have (or triggered by scale)

| Policy | When |
|---|---|
| **Recruitment & Selection Policy** | Once you're hiring with any regularity — ensures consistency and documents that your process is non-discriminatory. |
| **Learning & Development Policy** | You listed Personal Development — this is the broader framework it sits in. |
| **Modern Slavery Statement** | Required by statute only above £36m turnover. Not applicable yet, but worth knowing the trigger. |

## 1. Personal Development Policy

### 1. Purpose & scope
- What it governs: how the company supports employees in developing skills, knowledge, and career progression — both for their benefit and the business's.
- Audience: all employees (and, if applicable, contractors — state which).
- Sits alongside the performance management framework; development is the positive counterpart to underperformance management.
- Distinct from the *benefits* policy (though the two overlap where development spend is a benefit).

### 2. Regulatory drivers
- **None are strictly REQUIRED** for this policy — there is no statutory right to personal development time or spend.
- **Employment Rights Act 1996 [BEST PRACTICE]:** contracts must specify training entitlements (or lack thereof); if you promise development in the contract, the policy defines what that means.
- **Equality Act 2010 [REQUIRED consideration]:** development opportunities must be offered fairly. If the policy creates a discretionary budget, document how decisions are made to avoid indirect discrimination claims.
- **Apprenticeship Levy [FLAG]:** once your annual payroll exceeds £3m, you pay the Levy and can draw down funds for apprenticeships. Worth knowing the threshold.

### 3. Recommended sections

**Who can request and when** [any employee after probation]
> After probation? From day one? On a rolling basis or in annual reviews? Define eligibility clearly — discretion without a framework invites discrimination claims.

**What the company will fund** [£250 per year per head but will consider case-by-case requests]
> Courses, conferences, professional memberships, books, mentoring. Set either a per-head budget (e.g. £500/year) or a case-by-case approval process. Both work; vagueness doesn't.

**Study/exam leave** [No]
> Whether you pay employees while they study for external qualifications. Most early-stage companies do not, but not saying so leads to assumptions.

**Approval process** [Founder/Director until such time there is enough cause for senior managers]
> Who approves (line manager, founder), what lead time is needed, how requests are submitted.

**Clawback clause** [None - hate them.]
> If the company funds a qualification costing over a threshold (say £500), can it reclaim a proportion if the employee leaves within 12–24 months? This is a [REQUIRED legal input] because clawback provisions can be void as unlawful deductions from wages if not drafted correctly.

**What the company expects in return** [BEST PRACTICE]
> Knowledge-sharing, applying skills in role, or simply completing the course. Keep this proportionate — it's a carrot, not a stick.

**How it links to performance reviews** [BEST PRACTICE]
> Development plans should feed into annual/half-year reviews. Say so.

### 4. Marketplace-specific considerations
At Groomr's current size (small, pre-revenue), the practical risk here is that development spend is informal and undocumented. The policy is primarily about:
- Setting fair, defensible criteria so a future claim ("I was denied training because of X protected characteristic") can be rebutted.
- Being honest about clawback so employees know the deal before they accept funding.

### 5. Animal-welfare considerations
Not applicable to this policy.

### 6. Cross-references
- Performance Management Policy (development plans as part of review cycle)
- Employee Benefits Policy (if a development budget is framed as a benefit)
- Expenses Policy (how development costs are claimed)

### 7. Risk flags — get a solicitor on these
- **Clawback provisions** must be drafted as a specific contractual clause (ideally in the employment contract, not just the policy) to be enforceable. A policy statement alone is not sufficient. A wrongly drafted clause can be an unlawful deduction from wages under Part II ERA 1996. -- I DO NOT WANT ONE

### 8. Decisions needed from you
- Is there a per-head budget, or case-by-case approval?
- Do you pay for study leave (time off to sit exams, attend lectures)?
- Is there a clawback threshold, and over what period?
- Who approves requests — line manager, or founder sign-off on anything?

## 2. Employee Benefits Policy

### 1. Purpose & scope
- What it governs: the full schedule of non-salary benefits available to employees — what they are, who is eligible, and how they work.
- Audience: all employees. Distinguish what's available from day one vs. after probation.
- Separate from statutory entitlements (those must be provided; this covers what you give *above* that floor).
- Contractually important: benefits stated in a contract (or implied by consistent practice) can create binding entitlements. The policy needs to align with contracts precisely.

### 2. Regulatory drivers
- **Employment Rights Act 1996 [REQUIRED]:** written statement of particulars (day-one rights) must include details of any benefits. The policy fleshes out what the contract summarises.
- **Equality Act 2010 [REQUIRED]:** benefits must be offered on non-discriminatory terms. Part-time and fixed-term employees are entitled to comparable benefits pro-rata (Part-Time Workers Regs 2000, Fixed-Term Employees Regs 2002).
- **Income Tax (PAYE) / HMRC [REQUIRED]:** most non-cash benefits are taxable as benefits-in-kind — you'll need P11D reporting or payroll inclusion. Salary sacrifice (for pension, cycle-to-work, etc.) has specific HMRC rules.
- **Pensions Act 2008 / Auto-Enrolment [REQUIRED]:** eligible workers must be auto-enrolled into a qualifying pension scheme. This is the one benefit that is legally mandated.

### 3. Recommended sections

**Pension and auto-enrolment** [REQUIRED]
> Which pension provider, contribution rates (employer and employee), opt-out procedure. The minimum employer contribution is currently 3% of qualifying earnings. Note: when and if you set up a salary-sacrifice pension arrangement, get HMRC-compliant documentation.

**Holiday entitlement** [REQUIRED — see Holidays Policy for full detail]
> The benefits policy should cross-reference the holidays policy rather than duplicate it.

**Private medical / dental insurance** [COMMERCIAL CHOICE]
> If offered: who's eligible, whether dependants are included, waiting periods, what happens on exit. If not offered, state so — avoids assumption.

**Life assurance / death in service** [COMMERCIAL CHOICE]
> Common at even small companies (typically 2–4× salary). Explain the trust structure (death-in-service is normally written in trust, so it doesn't form part of the taxable estate — worth explaining to employees).

**Cycle to Work scheme** [BEST PRACTICE]
> HMRC-approved salary sacrifice. Popular, low cost to administer. Flag the requirement to use an approved provider and the credit-agreement cap.

**Employee Assistance Programme (EAP)** [BEST PRACTICE]
> Counselling and support line. Very cheap for employers (£5–10/head/year), significant mental-health obligation signposting. Useful to document given employer's duty to consider mental health under H&S legislation.

**Equity / share options** [COMMERCIAL CHOICE — with legal input]
> If you intend to offer EMI options (the HMRC-advantaged scheme for qualifying companies), this is where you reference the scheme rules. EMI options are a *separate legal document* — the policy just signals they exist and how employees learn more.

**Equipment and home-working allowance** [CROSS-REFERENCE]
> Point to the Use of Company Equipment and Remote Working policies rather than restating here.

**Perks (informal)** [COMMERCIAL CHOICE]
> Flexible hours, dog-friendly office, team events, etc. Be careful: if you document informal perks as policy they become harder to withdraw. Either list them as discretionary or don't put them in the policy.

### 4. Marketplace-specific considerations
As a two-sided marketplace, be careful your benefits policy doesn't inadvertently inform or be applied to groomers — they are not employees, and offering them any employment-adjacent benefits could feed a worker-classification argument.

- Use of Company Equipment Policy

- Auto-enrolment compliance — HMRC and The Pensions Regulator. Non-compliance fines are on a fixed-penalty basis regardless of company size.
- EMI options require HMRC valuation and scheme documentation before grant — not something to improvise.

## 3. Holidays Policy

### 1. Purpose & scope
- What it governs: annual leave entitlement, how it's requested and approved, bank holidays, carry-over, and pay during leave.
- Audience: all employees.
- Statutory floor exists in law; this policy documents whether and how you exceed it, and the mechanics of the process.

### 2. Regulatory drivers
- **Working Time Regulations 1998 [REQUIRED]:** 5.6 weeks (28 days including bank holidays for a full-time employee) statutory minimum. Cannot be contracted out of. Holiday pay must reflect "normal pay" — including regular overtime, commission, and allowances (a long line of case law; confirm with a solicitor if your pay structures are complex).
- **Employment Rights Act 1996 [REQUIRED]:** employees must be able to exercise statutory leave rights; dismissal for taking statutory leave is automatically unfair.
- **Part-Time Workers Regulations 2000 [REQUIRED]:** part-timers get pro-rata entitlement.
- **Working Time (Amendment) Regulations 2023 [REQUIRED — verify current status]:** introduced rolled-up holiday pay for irregular-hours workers and new carryover rules for workers who couldn't take leave due to sickness. Important for anyone on zero-hours or variable-hours arrangements.

### 3. Recommended sections

**Statutory entitlement and any contractual enhancement** [REQUIRED]
> State the number of days clearly (e.g. "25 days plus 8 bank holidays" if you're going above statutory). Distinguish the statutory 28-day minimum from any additional contractual entitlement — the rules on carry-over and payment on termination differ.

**Bank holidays** [COMMERCIAL CHOICE]
> Whether bank holidays come out of the above entitlement or are in addition. Most employers in Scotland treat them as "in addition" to a headline day allowance. Note: Scotland has slightly different bank holidays from England & Wales (2 January, St Andrew's Day). For a remote-first or mixed team, decide whether everyone follows the same schedule.

**Holiday year** [REQUIRED practical]
> Start and end date of the holiday year (often 1 January–31 December or hire-anniversary). Pro-rata calculation for joiners/leavers flows from this.

**How to book leave** [BEST PRACTICE]
> Minimum notice (typically 2× the leave requested), approval process, blackout periods (e.g. product launch sprints — flag that business-need refusal must be handled carefully to avoid discrimination claims if it falls on a religious festival date).

**Carry-over** [REQUIRED — limited, with conditions]
> Statutory 4 weeks cannot be carried over except in specific circumstances (sickness, family leave, parental leave) under Working Time Regs. The additional 1.6 statutory weeks and any contractual enhancement above that: your call. Many employers allow a capped carry-over (e.g. 5 days) to avoid a "year-end rush". State the rules clearly.

**Payment on termination** [REQUIRED]
> Any accrued but untaken holiday must be paid on termination (for statutory entitlement). State how you calculate the daily rate.

**Holiday during notice period** [BEST PRACTICE]
> Employer can require employees to take outstanding holiday during notice. State this so it's expected.

**Long-service additional leave** [COMMERCIAL CHOICE]
> Some companies add a day per year of service up to a cap. If you want this, define it here.

### 4. Marketplace-specific considerations
If you ever use contractors or freelancers, they are typically not entitled to WTR holiday pay — but *worker* status (which some freelancers may claim) does carry WTR rights. Don't import this policy for non-employees without checking status.

### 5. Animal-welfare considerations
Not applicable.

### 6. Cross-references
- Sickness Policy (interaction between sickness and holiday accrual during sick leave)
- Parental Leave Policy (interaction between maternity/paternity leave and holiday accrual)
- Employee Benefits Policy (total leave package headline)

### 7. Risk flags — get a solicitor on these
- **Holiday pay calculation** for employees receiving commission, regular overtime, or variable allowances is a hotly litigated area. The Bear Scotland/Flowers line of cases changed what must be included. Get this right before you hire anyone with variable pay.
- Carry-over rules post-sickness changed with the WTR Amendment 2023 — check the current position applies correctly to your holiday year structure.

- Separate bank holidays or included in the headline figure? included
- Which bank holidays: Scottish calendar, English calendar, or employee's choice? Scottish
- Carry-over: none, capped (how many days?), or full roll? 3

## 4. Sickness Policy

### 1. Purpose & scope
- What it governs: how sickness absence is managed — reporting, Statutory Sick Pay (SSP), any enhanced company sick pay, return-to-work, and when absence triggers a formal process.
- Audience: all employees.
- Covers short-term/frequent absence and long-term absence separately — they need different management approaches.

### 2. Regulatory drivers
- **Statutory Sick Pay Act 1994 / Social Security Contributions and Benefits Act 1992 [REQUIRED]:** SSP (currently £116.75/week, April 2025 rate) kicks in after 3 qualifying waiting days. You must pay it to eligible employees; you cannot opt out. The policy documents your process but the statutory floor is non-negotiable.
- **Employment Rights Act 1996 [REQUIRED]:** dismissal for sickness can be fair (capability) but only if you follow a fair process — obtaining medical evidence, considering reasonable adjustments, allowing the employee to respond. Without a documented process you're exposed.
- **Equality Act 2010 [REQUIRED]:** long-term illness may be a *disability* (if it has a substantial and long-term adverse effect on ability to carry out normal day-to-day activities). From that point, you have a duty to make reasonable adjustments. Dismissal without doing so is discriminatory. Your sickness policy *must* flag this trigger.
- **Access to Medical Reports Act 1988 [REQUIRED]:** if you want an employee's GP or specialist to provide a report, you must comply with the Act — the employee consents and has the right to see and amend the report.
- **General Data Protection Regulation (UK GDPR) [REQUIRED]:** sickness records are *special-category health data* — higher lawful-basis requirements (typically *substantial public interest* or *employment law obligation*), explicit data minimisation, and retention limits apply.

### 3. Recommended sections

**Reporting procedure** [REQUIRED in practice]
> Who the employee contacts (line manager, HR), by when (first thing on day one of absence), and how (call, not text). A Bradford Factor or trigger-point system starts here.

**Self-certification period** [REQUIRED]
> Employees can self-certify for up to 7 calendar days. After that, a fit note from a GP is required for SSP eligibility. State this clearly.

**Statutory Sick Pay** [REQUIRED]
> SSP rate, waiting days, duration (up to 28 weeks), what happens when SSP ends.

**Company sick pay (CSP) — if any** [COMMERCIAL CHOICE]
> Full pay / half pay for a defined period before dropping to SSP. State the eligibility criteria (e.g. after probation, accrues with service). This is your most expensive decision in this policy.

**Fit-to-work notes and phased returns** [BEST PRACTICE]
> From April 2010, fit notes replaced sick notes — a GP can certify someone "may be fit for work" with adjustments. State that phased returns, adjusted duties, and reduced hours will be considered.

**Return-to-work interviews** [BEST PRACTICE — significant risk mitigation]
> A brief meeting on every return from absence. Statistically the single most effective deterrent to casual short-term absence and the first formal step in addressing a pattern. Strongly recommended.

**Trigger points and the Bradford Factor** [COMMERCIAL CHOICE]
> Some employers use the Bradford Factor (frequency × frequency × total days) to flag persistent short-term absence. If you use it, document it — applying it without disclosure is unfair. Decide whether to use it or not; it's not mandatory.

**Long-term absence and the Equality Act intersection** [REQUIRED]
> At what point you seek Occupational Health (OH) or GP report; the duty to consider reasonable adjustments; the process before any capability dismissal for long-term sickness. This is the section most likely to generate a tribunal claim if absent or vague.

**Mental health** [BEST PRACTICE]
> Explicit recognition that mental-health conditions are treated the same as physical conditions. Mention the EAP. This section matters for culture as much as law.

### 4. Marketplace-specific considerations
Not applicable to this policy (purely internal HR).

### 5. Animal-welfare considerations
Not applicable.

### 6. Cross-references
- Holidays Policy (holiday accrues during sick leave; SSP/holiday overlap rules)
- Employee Benefits Policy (EAP — first point of support)
- Disciplinary & Grievance Policy (persistent absence management crosses into that territory)
- Parental Leave Policy (different rules apply when sickness coincides with maternity leave)

### 7. Risk flags — get a solicitor on these
- **The Equality Act disability threshold** is not a bright line — "long-term" means likely to last 12 months or recur, and "substantial" is interpreted broadly by tribunals. Sacking someone too early for sickness, without proper adjustments, is one of the most common employment tribunal claims. Get the process signed off.
- **Company sick pay interaction with SSP** — if your CSP scheme doesn't spell out its relationship to SSP, HMRC can challenge whether you've met your SSP obligations.

### 8. Decisions needed from you
- Will you offer CSP above SSP? At what rate and for how long (e.g. 4 weeks full, 4 weeks half)? 6 weeks / 6 weeks
- After how long does CSP kick in (day one, or after probation)? after probation
- Will you use a Bradford Factor / trigger points? If yes, at what score? TBC
- Will you have a formal Occupational Health referral pathway, or handle informally at this stage? Informal

## 5. Remote Working Policy

### 1. Purpose & scope
- What it governs: arrangements for working outside the company's main office — whether full-time remote, hybrid, or ad-hoc home working.
- Audience: all employees.
- Critical to define: "remote working" as a *permanent arrangement* vs. "working from home" on an ad-hoc basis — the legal and operational implications differ.
- Since April 2024, employees can request flexible working (including remote) from day one — this policy sets the framework within which those requests are granted or refused.

### 2. Regulatory drivers
- **Employment Relations (Flexible Working) Act 2023 [REQUIRED]:** right to request flexible working from day one; up to two requests per year; must be handled within two months; eight permissible business reasons for refusal; duty to consult before refusing.
- **Health and Safety at Work Act 1974 / DSE Regulations 1992 [REQUIRED]:** display-screen equipment (DSE) assessment required for any employee regularly using a screen at home. "Regularly" means habitually, not just occasionally. You must provide or arrange an assessment.
- **Employer's Liability (Compulsory Insurance) Act 1969 [REQUIRED]:** your EL insurance must extend to home-working employees. Check with your broker — some policies require explicit notification.
- **UK GDPR + DPA 2018 [REQUIRED]:** home workers processing personal data (which includes your employee data, user data, etc.) must do so securely. The policy must address this.
- **Income Tax (Expenses) [BEST PRACTICE — verify with accountant]:** HMRC allows a £6/week tax-free home-working allowance where an employee is *required* to work from home. Whether you pay this through payroll or the employee claims it directly depends on whether remote working is contractual or ad-hoc.

### 3. Recommended sections

**Who is eligible and how remote arrangements are agreed** [REQUIRED — links to flexible working right]
> Is remote working contractual (written into employment contracts) or discretionary? The answer changes the employee's right to insist on it. Contractual remote working is harder to withdraw.

**Core hours and availability expectations** [COMMERCIAL CHOICE]
> For an async/startup environment, define what "available" means — whether that's Slack response within X hours, core hours of 10am–3pm, or something else. Absence of guidance leads to misaligned expectations.

**DSE self-assessment** [REQUIRED]
> All home-working employees must complete a DSE assessment (or you must arrange one). Provide the form or link. Include minimum workspace requirements (desk, chair, adequate lighting).

**Equipment provision** [COMMERCIAL CHOICE — see also Use of Equipment Policy]
> Who provides what (laptop, monitor, keyboard, chair allowance). If you provide equipment, cross-reference the Use of Equipment Policy for acceptable use and return obligations.

**Data security at home** [REQUIRED — UK GDPR]
> Password-protected home Wi-Fi, VPN if applicable, no use of personal cloud storage for work files, clear-desk equivalent for screen visibility, printer/paper disposal. This is the highest-risk section legally.

**Expenses and allowances** [CROSS-REFERENCE]
> Point to the Expenses Policy for home-working allowances rather than restating here. Confirm whether you pay the HMRC £6/week flat rate.

**Insurance and liability** [REQUIRED — brief]
> Employees should check their home contents insurance (some policies exclude business use of equipment at home). State that EL insurance extends to the home workplace.

**Tax and employment status of remote workers** [COMMERCIAL CHOICE — with accountant input]
> If any employee is based overseas (even temporarily — "working from the villa for two weeks"), this triggers complex tax, employment law, and social security issues in the host country. State clearly whether working abroad is permitted and if so, the approval process.

**Review and withdrawal of remote arrangements** [COMMERCIAL CHOICE — legal input needed]
> If the arrangement is discretionary, reserve the right to require return to office with reasonable notice. If contractual, withdrawal is a change to terms requiring consent. State which applies.

### 4. Marketplace-specific considerations
As Groomr scales, remote employees processing user data (bookings, payment data, vet records) are a GDPR risk point. The data security section of this policy is where you demonstrate Article 32 compliance (appropriate technical and organisational measures).

### 5. Animal-welfare considerations
Not applicable to this policy.

### 6. Cross-references
- Use of Company Equipment Policy (hardware provided for home use)
- Expenses Policy (home-working allowance)
- Disciplinary & Grievance Policy (remote-worker conduct and monitoring)
- Employee Privacy Notice (monitoring of remote workers — see below)

### 7. Risk flags — get a solicitor on these
- **Monitoring of remote workers** — if you use any activity-monitoring software (screen capture, keylogging, call recording) you must comply with UK GDPR, RIPA 2000, and the ICO's Employee Monitoring guidance (updated 2023). A policy statement alone is insufficient — you need a documented DPIA (Data Protection Impact Assessment). Monitoring without disclosure is a criminal offence under RIPA.
- **Working-abroad requests** — UK employment law protects employees who work abroad if they retain strong connection to GB. But the host country's law may *also* apply. Get jurisdiction-specific advice for any overseas arrangement lasting more than a few weeks.
- **Contractual vs. discretionary remote working** — once a remote arrangement has been in place for an extended period without challenge, a tribunal may find it has become an implied contractual term. Regular written confirmation that arrangements are discretionary helps prevent this.

## 6. Travel for Work Policy

### 1. Purpose & scope
- What it governs: travel required for work purposes — how it is authorised, booked, what class of travel is acceptable, and the duty-of-care obligations when employees are travelling.
- Audience: all employees who travel for work.
- Distinct from commuting (not a work expense) and remote working (covered separately).
- Note: Groomr is currently a small, primarily digital business. Anticipate travel for conferences, investor meetings, and on-site visits to groomer partners.

### 2. Regulatory drivers
- **Health and Safety at Work Act 1974 [REQUIRED]:** employer's duty of care extends to employees travelling for work, including risk assessments for high-risk travel and 24-hour emergency contact.
- **Working Time Regulations 1998 [REQUIRED consideration]:** travel time to a non-regular workplace counts as working time. If it pushes employees over the 48-hour week average, you need to manage that.
- **HMRC travel and subsistence rules [REQUIRED — financial]:** reimbursement of travel/subsistence is tax and NI-free only if it meets HMRC's qualifying criteria. The mileage rate (currently 45p/mile for first 10,000 miles, 25p thereafter), the rules on "irregular place of work", and meal subsistence rates must be documented to support expense claims.
- **Corporate Manslaughter and Corporate Homicide Act 2007 [REQUIRED awareness]:** extends corporate liability to fatal accidents during work travel if there is a gross breach in the way activities are managed. A policy with clear driving/fatigue rules is part of the paper trail.

### 3. Recommended sections

**Authorisation process** [BEST PRACTICE]
> Who approves travel, minimum lead time, whether a business case is needed above a cost threshold.

**Booking travel** [COMMERCIAL CHOICE]
> DIY via expenses, a central booking tool, or designated travel agents. State class of travel (e.g. economy for flights under 3 hours, standard class rail — or specify thresholds where business class is permissible).

**Accommodation** [COMMERCIAL CHOICE]
> Budget cap per night by city. Whether Airbnb is permitted (HMRC has views on this for tax; some insurance policies exclude it too).

**Driving for work** [REQUIRED — duty of care]
> Must hold a valid licence; duty to report endorsements. If using a personal vehicle, must have business-use insurance (standard personal policies exclude this). Keep a journey-time log to address fatigue. Mileage rate (HMRC approved amount).

**Duty of care and emergency contacts** [REQUIRED]
> The company must know where travelling employees are and be contactable in an emergency. State the procedure, the emergency contact number, and what to do if plans change significantly.

**International travel** [BEST PRACTICE]
> FCDO travel advice check before travel, health insurance/travel insurance provision, visa/right-to-work checks for the destination, data security (border inspection of devices).

**Sustainability / carbon footprint** [COMMERCIAL CHOICE]
> Rail over flights for sub-3-hour journeys, carbon-offset options. Optional but increasingly relevant for employer branding.

## 7. Expenses Policy

### 1. Purpose & scope
- What it governs: how employees claim reimbursement for expenditure incurred on company business — what is and isn't claimable, what evidence is required, and the process and timescales for reimbursement.
- Audience: all employees and, if applicable, contractors (state separately — different tax treatment applies).
- One of the highest practical sources of day-to-day friction and one of the most common areas where HMRC inquiries start if it's sloppy.

### 2. Regulatory drivers
- **Income Tax (Earnings and Pensions) Act 2003 / HMRC P11D rules [REQUIRED]:** reimbursed expenses are only tax/NI-free if they are "wholly, exclusively and necessarily" incurred in the performance of employment duties. Anything outside this is a benefit-in-kind requiring P11D reporting (or payrolling of benefits). The policy's approved-expense categories must track this.
- **HMRC Approved Mileage Allowance Payments (AMAPs) [REQUIRED]:** 45p/mile up to 10,000 miles per year, 25p thereafter. Payment at these rates is tax and NI-free without a P11D. Above AMAP rates, the excess is taxable. Document this explicitly.
- **VAT [REQUIRED for VAT-registered companies]:** VAT can be reclaimed on most employee expense claims, but the receipt must be a proper VAT receipt (showing supplier VAT number, amount of VAT). Train employees on what counts. Once Groomr is VAT-registered, this matters.
- **Money Laundering Regs [REQUIRED — low-level but real]:** unusual expense claims with no receipts can be an indicator. Not a major risk at Groomr's scale, but document the "original receipt required" rule.

### 3. Recommended sections

**What can and cannot be claimed** [REQUIRED]
> An explicit list. Claimable: travel (rail, bus, parking, business-purpose flights, AMAP mileage), accommodation, meals while away (subsistence — typically £5 breakfast, £10 lunch, £25 dinner, or similar internal thresholds), business entertainment (with HMRC-compliant record of attendees and business purpose), work-related subscriptions, equipment up to a threshold. Not claimable: commuting, personal entertainment, alcohol above a modest subsistence allowance, fines and penalties.

**Business entertainment — the HMRC trap** [REQUIRED — commonly misunderstood]
> Entertaining clients/customers is *not* a qualifying HMRC expense (employer cannot deduct it as a business expense and it's a BIK to the employee if the employer pays). Entertaining employees is allowable up to the £150/head annual staff events exemption. Document both rules — they catch people out constantly.

**Receipts and evidence** [REQUIRED]
> Original receipt required for all claims above £10 (or set your own threshold). What counts as an acceptable receipt (VAT invoice vs. card receipt vs. email confirmation). Many companies now accept photos of receipts via expense software — state if so.

**Pre-approval thresholds** [COMMERCIAL CHOICE]
> Anything above £[X] requires prior sign-off. Avoids nasty surprises.

**Submission process and timescales** [BEST PRACTICE]
> How claims are submitted (expense software, spreadsheet, email), by when (typically monthly, within 30 days of incurring), and how quickly reimbursement is made (payroll cycle, or faster for large amounts).

**Corporate card / company card (if applicable)** [COMMERCIAL CHOICE]
> Rules for use of a company credit card: only business expenses, personal spend must be reimbursed immediately, reconciliation process.

**Out-of-pocket cash advances** [COMMERCIAL CHOICE]
> Whether the company advances cash for anticipated expenses and how the advance is reconciled.

**Consequences of non-compliance** [REQUIRED — for enforceability]
> False or inflated expense claims is gross misconduct leading to summary dismissal. Stating this explicitly makes the disciplinary process cleaner.

### 4. Marketplace-specific considerations
If Groomr employees ever spend on platform marketing, partner acquisition, or groomer-relationship activities, the business-entertainment rules above apply — client meals require names and business purpose documented at the time, not retrospectively.

## 8. Use of Company Equipment Policy

### 1. Purpose & scope
- What it governs: assets provided to employees for work — primarily laptops, phones, and peripherals, but also software licences, access credentials, and any physical tools or equipment.
- Audience: all employees who are issued company equipment.
- Covers both what employees may and may not do with equipment, and the process for return on termination.

### 2. Regulatory drivers
- **UK GDPR + DPA 2018 [REQUIRED]:** devices used for work are likely to contain personal data. Article 32 requires "appropriate technical and organisational measures" — full-disk encryption, screen locks, and remote-wipe capability are the minimum technical baseline. The policy is part of the organisational measure.
- **Computer Misuse Act 1990 [REQUIRED awareness]:** unauthorised access to computer systems is a criminal offence. The policy defines the boundary of "authorised" use for employees and for third parties.
- **Investigatory Powers Act 2016 / RIPA 2000 + ICO Employee Monitoring guidance [REQUIRED if you monitor]:** monitoring employee use of company devices must be disclosed, proportionate, and documented in a DPIA. The policy is the disclosure document.
- **Theft Act 1968 (E&W) / Theft by Hacking (Scotland — common law) [REQUIRED awareness]:** equipment is company property; removing it without permission is theft. The return-on-termination section creates the legal basis for recovering equipment.

### 3. Recommended sections

**What is covered** [REQUIRED]
> List asset types: laptops, monitors, phones, peripherals, SaaS licences, access credentials (GitHub, Supabase, Stripe, Cloudinary, Clerk dashboards — all critical for Groomr). Make clear that access credentials are company assets even though they're not physical objects.

**Acceptable use — personal use** [COMMERCIAL CHOICE]
> Most employers permit reasonable personal use (checking personal email, brief browsing). The line is explicit: no illegal content, no accessing of personal accounts using company credentials, no downloading personal software that could introduce malware. State whether personal use during work hours is restricted (e.g. limited to breaks).

**Security requirements** [REQUIRED — UK GDPR]
> Full-disk encryption enabled (FileVault on macOS, BitLocker on Windows — both supported and the employee must confirm it is on).
> Screen-lock after maximum 10 minutes idle.
> VPN use when on public Wi-Fi for any work activity.
> Multi-factor authentication on all work accounts (this is non-negotiable for a platform that holds payment and personal data).
> Prohibition on saving work files to personal cloud storage (personal iCloud, Google Drive, Dropbox).

**Software installation** [COMMERCIAL CHOICE]
> Whether employees can install software independently or require IT approval. For a small team, a whitelist approach is impractical; a "do not install anything that poses security risk" rule with clear examples is more workable.

**Asset register and reporting loss/damage** [REQUIRED]
> All company equipment is logged on an asset register (serial numbers, who it's assigned to). If equipment is lost, stolen, or damaged, report immediately — a lost device containing personal data is a reportable data breach if you cannot confirm it was encrypted.

**Return on termination** [REQUIRED]
> All equipment must be returned on the last day or by a specified date. State what happens if it is not returned (deduction from final pay — note: only permissible if there is a written contractual clause; cannot be in the policy alone). Outline the remote-wipe process.

**Access revocation** [REQUIRED — operationally critical for a tech company]
> On the last day of employment, all system access (GitHub, Supabase, Stripe, Cloudinary, Clerk, email, Slack) is revoked. For a company like Groomr where a departing employee could access live production systems, this is as important as the physical return. Document who is responsible and the checklist.

**Monitoring** [REQUIRED if applicable — with legal input]
> If you use any device management software (MDM), activity logging, or screen monitoring: state what is monitored, why, and that employees consent by using company equipment. Link to the DPIA.

### 4. Marketplace-specific considerations
This is one of the most Groomr-critical policies in this list. An engineer with a compromised or unencrypted laptop has access to Supabase (all user data), Stripe (financial data), Clerk (authentication), and Cloudinary. A lost unencrypted device is a mandatory ICO breach notification (72-hour window). The security requirements section here must be technically enforced, not just stated.

### 5. Animal-welfare considerations
Not applicable.

### 6. Cross-references

- Employee Privacy Notice (monitoring disclosures)
- Disciplinary & Grievance Policy (gross misconduct for deliberate misuse or failure to return)
- Expenses Policy (if employees buy equipment and claim it back rather than receiving company-owned kit)

### 7. Risk flags — get a solicitor on these
- **Wage deduction for unreturned equipment** can only be made if there is a *written contractual clause* expressly permitting it — a policy statement is insufficient under Part II ERA 1996. The deduction clause must be in the employment contract.
- **Data breach from lost device** — if you cannot confirm the device was encrypted, you must self-report to the ICO within 72 hours. You cannot wait to see if the device is recovered. The policy must make encryption non-optional, not just recommended.
- **Access revocation on termination** — a disgruntled departing employee retaining live production credentials is a Computer Misuse Act risk as well as a data breach risk. Put a hard process and a checklist around this.
