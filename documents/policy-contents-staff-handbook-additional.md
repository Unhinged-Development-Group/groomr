## Policy 1: Disciplinary & Grievance Policy

### 1. Purpose & scope
- Governs the process for managing conduct and performance concerns (disciplinary) and for employees to raise formal concerns about their treatment at work (grievance).
- Applies to all employees. Does not apply to contractors, freelancers, or (critically for Groomr) independent groomers on the platform.
- Sits alongside the Performance Management Policy: the disciplinary procedure handles misconduct; the capability procedure handles underperformance. Keep them in separate tracks — conflating the two is a common route to unfair dismissal.
- Must be given to employees as part of their written statement of particulars on day one (Employment Rights Act 1996 s.1).

### 2. Regulatory drivers
- **ACAS Code of Practice on Disciplinary and Grievance Procedures 2015 [REQUIRED in effect]:** not statute, but Employment Tribunals have a statutory power (TULRCA 1992 s.207A) to adjust awards by up to 25% for unreasonable failure to follow it. Draft as if it is binding because practically it is.
- **Employment Rights Act 1996 [REQUIRED]:** right to written reasons for dismissal; right to notice; what makes a dismissal fair (capability, conduct, redundancy, statutory restriction, some other substantial reason). Procedural fairness is a separate requirement from substantive fairness — you can have a good reason and still lose a tribunal claim on process alone.
- **Employment Relations Act 1999 [REQUIRED]:** right to be accompanied at a disciplinary or grievance hearing by a trade union representative or work colleague. This is a day-one right regardless of length of service.
- **Equality Act 2010 [REQUIRED]:** disciplinary action taken disproportionately against employees with a protected characteristic, or grievances raising discrimination, engage the Act. The procedure must be capable of handling both.

### 3. Recommended sections

**Scope and principles** [REQUIRED]
> State the purpose (fair, consistent, transparent process; not punitive). Confirm the right to be accompanied. State that the procedure does not alter the employment contract.

**Informal resolution** [BEST PRACTICE — but required by ACAS Code spirit]
> Before formal steps: a quiet word, informal coaching, management conversation. Many issues can and should be resolved here. Document that it happened. Skipping straight to formal is poor process.

**Investigations** [REQUIRED]
> Before any formal hearing: appoint an investigating manager (not the hearing manager), gather evidence, interview witnesses, produce an investigation report. The employee must not be prejudged. Suspension pending investigation: must be brief, non-punitive, on full pay, reviewed regularly — suspension ≠ guilt.

**Formal disciplinary hearing** [REQUIRED]
> Written invitation with: allegation(s), evidence, date, right-to-companion notice, reasonable advance notice (typically 5+ working days). Hearing procedure: employee can respond, call witnesses, present evidence. Decision: no action, first written warning, final written warning, dismissal (with or without notice).

**Gross misconduct — illustrative list** [REQUIRED — list, not exhaustive]
> Examples (must be illustrative, not exhaustive): theft/fraud, violence, serious breach of health and safety, unauthorised disclosure of confidential data, harassment of a colleague, being unfit for work due to alcohol/drugs, serious dishonesty, deliberate damage to company property. Flag: the list informs what "gross" means — it does not prevent other conduct qualifying.

**Warnings — duration and live period** [BEST PRACTICE]
> First written warning: typically 6–12 months live. Final written warning: 12 months live. After expiry, the warning lapses and cannot be relied upon as a stepping-stone to dismissal. State durations explicitly.

**Dismissal with and without notice** [REQUIRED]
> Gross misconduct = summary dismissal (no notice). Other conduct after final warning = dismissal with contractual/statutory notice. Payment in lieu of notice (PILON): only lawful if the employment contract includes a PILON clause — without one, paying PILON is technically a breach of contract (albeit one the employee would accept). Get this clause into the contract, not just the policy.

**Appeals** [REQUIRED — ACAS Code]
> Every disciplinary decision must be appealable. Appeal to be heard by someone more senior than the original decision-maker, or at minimum a different manager if the company is small. Appeal does not automatically stay the decision. Outcome: uphold, vary, or overturn.

**Grievance procedure** [REQUIRED — mirror structure]
> Informal step first (same logic as disciplinary). Formal grievance: written complaint to line manager (or CEO/founder if complaint is about line manager). Hearing within a reasonable time (ACAS suggests 5 working days where possible). Written outcome. Right of appeal.

**Grievances raised during disciplinary proceedings** [REQUIRED — common trap]
> If an employee raises a grievance during a disciplinary process, consider whether the grievance is related. If related: both can be handled together or sequentially — state your approach. The disciplinary does not automatically pause, but fairness may require it.

**Record keeping** [REQUIRED — GDPR + ERA]
> All disciplinary records (investigation notes, hearing notes, outcome letters) are personal data and must be processed in accordance with the Employee Privacy Notice. Lapsed warnings should be removed from the file after the live period, or at minimum not be relied upon.

### 4. Marketplace-specific considerations
The groomers on the Groomr platform are not employees — this procedure does not apply to them. If a groomer behaves badly (e.g. abuses a user), the response mechanism is the platform's Acceptable Use / Community Standards policy, not this one. The risk is the opposite direction: an employee who also happens to use Groomr as a dog owner — keep platform and employment complaints in separate tracks.

### 5. Animal-welfare considerations
Not applicable to this policy.

### 6. Cross-references
- Performance Management Policy (capability track — distinct from misconduct)
- Equal Opportunities & Anti-Discrimination Policy (grievances alleging discrimination)
- Anti-Harassment & Bullying Policy (grievances alleging harassment)
- Whistleblowing Policy (if a grievance is actually a protected disclosure — different protections apply)
- Employee Privacy Notice (processing of disciplinary/grievance records)

### 7. Risk flags — get a solicitor on these
- **PILON clause** must be in the employment contract, not just the policy — check every contract template before the first hire.
- **Dismissal without procedure** during probation is still potentially unfair dismissal (if the employee has 2+ years' service — the threshold for unfair dismissal protection). But note: the 2-year threshold does not apply to automatically unfair dismissals (e.g. dismissal for whistleblowing, asserting a statutory right, or exercising a family-leave right). Those are protected from day one. Your disciplinary procedure must be capable of identifying these early.
- **Discrimination in disciplinary outcomes** — if data shows (even informally) that disciplinary action is taken more often against employees with a protected characteristic, that is indirect discrimination exposure. Keep records and review patterns periodically.

### 8. Decisions needed from you
- Warning durations: 6 months / 12 months / 18 months for first and final written warnings?
- Will the appeal be to a different manager, the board, or an external HR consultant at this stage?
- PILON: yes/no in the employment contract (determines whether summary PILON is an option)?
- At what headcount will you appoint a designated HR function vs. external HR support?

## Policy 2: Equal Opportunities & Anti-Discrimination Policy

### 1. Purpose & scope
- Governs the company's commitment to fair treatment in all aspects of employment: recruitment, pay, promotion, development, and exit.
- Applies to all employees and to the recruitment process (including candidates).
- Distinct from the Anti-Harassment & Bullying Policy (which covers ongoing conduct in the workplace) — this policy covers structural and systemic discrimination, not just individual incidents.
- Also applies (with a different lens) to the platform's treatment of groomers and owners as users — see section 4.

### 2. Regulatory drivers
- **Equality Act 2010 [REQUIRED]:** prohibits discrimination on nine protected characteristics: age, disability, gender reassignment, marriage and civil partnership, pregnancy and maternity, race, religion or belief, sex, and sexual orientation. Four prohibited conduct types: direct discrimination, indirect discrimination, harassment, victimisation. Employer is vicariously liable for acts of employees unless it took all reasonable steps to prevent them.
- **Part-Time Workers (Prevention of Less Favourable Treatment) Regs 2000 [REQUIRED]:** part-time employees must not be treated less favourably than comparable full-time employees without objective justification.
- **Fixed-Term Employees (Prevention of Less Favourable Treatment) Regs 2002 [REQUIRED]:** same principle for fixed-term employees.
- **Equality Act 2010 s.149 [BEST PRACTICE — not applicable to private companies directly, but good practice to reference]:** the Public Sector Equality Duty does not apply to Groomr as a private company, but the general principle of having due regard to equality in decisions is sound practice and increasingly expected by investors, partners, and regulators.
- **Pay Transparency [BEST PRACTICE — watch developing law]:** gender pay gap reporting is mandatory for employers with 250+ employees. Not applicable yet, but a pay-transparency habit now avoids issues at scale.

### 3. Recommended sections

**Statement of commitment** [BEST PRACTICE]
> Brief, non-waffy statement that the company is committed to equal opportunity and will not tolerate discrimination. This is the policy's anchor — it signals tone and creates the organisational expectation.

**The nine protected characteristics — defined** [REQUIRED]
> List and briefly define each. Not because employees don't know what "race" means, but because the legal definitions have nuances (e.g. "disability" under the Act requires substantial and long-term adverse effect on day-to-day activities; "belief" covers philosophical beliefs meeting the Grainger criteria). Brief, not academic.

**Prohibited conduct — four types** [REQUIRED]
> - Direct discrimination: treating someone less favourably because of a protected characteristic.
> - Indirect discrimination: a provision, criterion, or practice that applies to everyone but puts a protected group at a particular disadvantage, unless objectively justified.
> - Harassment: unwanted conduct related to a protected characteristic that has the purpose or effect of violating dignity or creating a hostile/degrading environment.
> - Victimisation: detrimental treatment of someone who has made or supported a discrimination complaint.

**Reasonable adjustments** [REQUIRED]
> Duty to make reasonable adjustments for disabled employees (and job applicants). State that employees should inform the company of any disability or health condition that may require adjustment, and that all requests will be considered positively. The duty applies to physical features of workplaces, provisions/criteria/practices, and the provision of auxiliary aids.

**Recruitment and selection** [REQUIRED]
> Job descriptions based on genuine role requirements. Selection criteria objective and applied consistently. Interview questions must not probe protected characteristics. Reasonable adjustments in the application and interview process. Monitoring of applicant demographic data (aggregate, anonymised) to identify patterns.

**Pay and benefits** [REQUIRED]
> Equal pay for equal work or work of equal value (Equality Act 2010 Part 5). If you have pay bands, document the rationale. Be wary of discretionary pay decisions that lack a paper trail — these are the single most common source of equal-pay claims.

**Promotion, development, and opportunity** [REQUIRED]
> Opportunities must be advertised internally and assessed fairly, not informally allocated. Document decisions.

**Pregnancy and maternity** [REQUIRED — specific protections]
> Distinct protections during pregnancy and maternity leave: the "protected period" during which unfavourable treatment is automatically discriminatory without requiring a comparator. State these protections explicitly because managers are frequently unaware of their scope.

**Reporting and investigation** [REQUIRED]
> How to report a discrimination concern (to line manager, or HR/founder if the line manager is implicated). Timeline for investigation. Use the Grievance Procedure for formal complaints. Protection from victimisation for making a report.

**Monitoring and review** [BEST PRACTICE]
> The policy should commit to periodic review of recruitment, pay, and development data to identify any patterns of disparity by protected characteristic. State the review cadence (annually is reasonable for a small company).

### 4. Marketplace-specific considerations
This policy is for employees. But Groomr also has a platform-level obligation to its users under the Equality Act: groomers cannot refuse service to pet owners on discriminatory grounds, and Groomr cannot discriminate in how it treats groomers or owners on the platform. These platform-level duties belong in the Terms of Service and Acceptable Use Policy — not this one. Keep them separate to avoid conflating employment law with platform terms.

### 5. Animal-welfare considerations
Not applicable.

### 6. Cross-references
- Anti-Harassment & Bullying Policy (conduct in the workplace)
- Disciplinary & Grievance Policy (formal complaints of discrimination)
- Recruitment & Selection Policy (when written)
- Parental Leave Policy (pregnancy and maternity protections)

### 7. Risk flags — get a solicitor on these
- **"All reasonable steps" defence** (Equality Act s.109(4)): this is the only available defence to vicarious liability. To rely on it, you need to have actually taken the steps — training, policy, reporting channels, investigation process. A policy alone is not sufficient; document the training too.
- **Equal pay** — even without a formal grievance, an equal-pay claim can be brought to an Employment Tribunal on behalf of a comparator. Keep pay decisions documented and defensible.

### 8. Decisions needed from you
- Will you run anonymised demographic data monitoring at hire? (Not mandatory for a small company but good practice.)
- External reporting channels for discrimination complaints — HR consultant, external solicitor, or internal only?
- Training: will new managers receive equality/discrimination training before managing? State the commitment in the policy even if the format is TBD.

## Policy 3: Anti-Harassment & Bullying Policy

### 1. Purpose & scope
- Governs the company's approach to preventing and addressing harassment, bullying, and victimisation in the workplace — including remote and digital workplaces.
- Applies to all employees in all work-related contexts: the office, client visits, team events, work-related social events, and online communications.
- Distinct from the Equal Opportunities Policy (which covers structural discrimination) and from the Disciplinary & Grievance Policy (which is the process mechanism). This policy defines what conduct is prohibited and how to report it.

### 2. Regulatory drivers
- **Equality Act 2010 s.26 [REQUIRED]:** defines harassment as unwanted conduct related to a protected characteristic that has the purpose or effect of violating dignity or creating a hostile, degrading, humiliating, or offensive environment. Three forms: general harassment, sexual harassment, and "less favourable treatment" following rejection of or submission to sexual harassment.
- **Worker Protection (Amendment of Equality Act 2010) Act 2023 [REQUIRED — from 26 October 2024]:** introduces a new *positive duty* on employers to take reasonable steps to **prevent** sexual harassment — not merely to respond to it. The EHRC has a statutory enforcement power against employers who fail to comply. This is the most significant legal change in this area in a decade. A policy document is necessary but may not be sufficient — the EHRC's statutory code expects risk assessments, monitoring, and training.
- **Health and Safety at Work Act 1974 [REQUIRED]:** the duty to provide a safe place of work includes protection from psychological harm caused by bullying. Bullying that causes psychiatric injury is a personal injury claim route (negligence) as well as an employment tribunal route.
- **Equality Act 2010 s.109 [REQUIRED for employer defence]:** "all reasonable steps" defence — the policy and associated training are the evidence base.

### 3. Recommended sections

**Definitions — harassment, bullying, sexual harassment, victimisation** [REQUIRED]
> Be precise. Use the statutory Equality Act definitions for harassment and sexual harassment. Bullying is not defined in statute — define it as repeated, unreasonable behaviour directed at an individual or group that creates a risk to health and safety (includes undermining, isolation, shouting, excessive workload as a weapon). Make clear that intent is irrelevant — the *effect* on the recipient is what matters.

**Examples of prohibited conduct** [BEST PRACTICE — illustrative only]
> Sexual harassment: unwanted touching, sexual comments or jokes, sharing explicit images, persistent asking for dates after refusal. Harassment on protected characteristics: racial slurs, mocking accents, derogatory comments about someone's age, religion, or disability. Bullying: public humiliation, excluding someone from meetings relevant to their role, overloading someone with work as a punishment. Cyber/digital: offensive messages, exclusion from work group chats, monitoring personal social media.

**Third-party harassment** [REQUIRED — Worker Protection Act 2023 extended to this]
> If employees are harassed by third parties (clients, contractors, visitors), the employer must take reasonable steps to protect the employee. For Groomr, this may be relevant if employees interact with platform users. State the procedure for reporting third-party harassment.

**The positive duty to prevent sexual harassment** [REQUIRED — Worker Protection Act 2023]
> This is now a separate, proactive obligation — not just a reactive one. State that the company will: assess the risk of sexual harassment in the workplace; implement and review preventive measures; provide regular training; ensure leadership sets a clear zero-tolerance tone. Document each of these steps independently of the policy — the EHRC can inspect compliance.

**How to report** [REQUIRED]
> To line manager (unless the line manager is the subject of the complaint, in which case to the next level, or to the founder). For complaints about senior leadership, state an alternative channel (external HR consultant, or direct to the board/co-founder). Document the route clearly — a policy that only routes complaints through the harasser is useless.

**Informal resolution** [COMMERCIAL CHOICE — with caveats]
> Some employees prefer to handle lower-level incidents informally (e.g. asking the person to stop, with support from a colleague). Document that this option exists but make clear the company will always take the matter seriously if the employee wants formal action. Never pressure someone to resolve harassment informally.

**Formal process** [REQUIRED]
> Routes into the Grievance Procedure for formal complaints. Investigation (separate from the disciplinary process). Outcome: substantiated → Disciplinary Procedure for the alleged harasser. State that both parties will be kept informed within confidentiality constraints.

**Support for complainants** [BEST PRACTICE]
> EAP access, confidentiality, protection from victimisation, the option to move teams or roles (where possible) during an investigation.

**False complaints** [COMMERCIAL CHOICE — with care]
> Malicious false complaints may be subject to the Disciplinary Procedure. State this carefully — it must not deter genuine complainants. The bar is "malicious and false", not "unproven". Get legal input on the drafting.

**Work-related social events** [BEST PRACTICE]
> Harassment at work-related social events (including team drinks) is covered by the policy. Brief managers to be aware of their obligations at these events. This is the single most common context for sexual harassment incidents.

### 4. Marketplace-specific considerations
Groomers on the platform are not employees. However, if an employee is harassed by a groomer or owner during a work interaction (e.g. a platform visit, a support call), this falls within third-party harassment obligations above. The platform's own Community Standards / Acceptable Use Policy handles user-to-user conduct.

### 5. Animal-welfare considerations
Not applicable.

### 6. Cross-references
- Equal Opportunities & Anti-Discrimination Policy (structural context)
- Disciplinary & Grievance Policy (process mechanism for both complainant grievance and alleged harasser disciplinary)
- Remote Working Policy (digital harassment, monitoring obligations)
- Social Media & Communications Policy (online harassment between colleagues)

### 7. Risk flags — get a solicitor on these
- **Worker Protection Act 2023 compliance** requires more than a policy — the EHRC's code expects a written risk assessment for sexual harassment, regular training, and documented monitoring. Engage an employment solicitor or EHRC-qualified HR consultant to review your full compliance picture, not just this document.
- **Work events liability** — employer liability for harassment at work-related social events is well established. Ensure your EL insurance extends to off-site events.
- **Drafting the false-complaints clause** — a clause that is too broad will deter reporting; one that is absent invites abuse. Get this clause specifically reviewed.

### 8. Decisions needed from you
- External reporting channel for complaints about senior management: HR consultant, external solicitor, or whistleblowing hotline?
- Training commitment: how often, and by whom delivered?
- Will you commission a sexual harassment risk assessment (the EHRC's code strongly suggests this is now expected)?
- Will you run anonymous staff surveys to monitor culture?

## Policy 4: Employee Privacy Notice

### 1. Purpose & scope
- This is the UK GDPR Article 13 transparency notice provided to employees at the point data is collected — typically at the start of employment (with the contract).
- Covers what personal data the company holds about employees, why it holds it, how long it keeps it, who it shares it with, and what employees' rights are.
- Audience: all employees. Consider whether a separate (shorter) notice is needed for job applicants.
- This is a *notice*, not a *policy* in the behavioural sense — it does not ask employees to comply with anything; it informs them of their rights. It can be a standalone document or a section of the employment contract, but it must be given before (or at the time of) first collection of personal data.

### 2. Regulatory drivers
- **UK GDPR Articles 13 & 14 [REQUIRED]:** mandatory transparency requirements — data subjects must be informed of: the controller's identity and contact details; the DPO's contact details if applicable; the purposes and lawful basis for processing; where the basis is legitimate interests, what those are; any recipients or categories of recipients; international transfers; retention periods; and all data subject rights.
- **Data Protection Act 2018 [REQUIRED]:** supplements UK GDPR for employment contexts, including the specific conditions for processing special-category data (health, biometrics, etc.) in the employment relationship.
- **Employment Rights Act 1996 [REQUIRED]:** the written statement of particulars must include information about any pension scheme, disciplinary and grievance procedures — these feed data collected by the company, so the privacy notice must cover them.
- **ICO's Employment Practices Code [BEST PRACTICE]:** the ICO's guidance (update post-UK GDPR) is not legally binding but sets the expected standard and will be used as a benchmark in any regulatory investigation.

### 3. Recommended sections

**Data controller identity** [REQUIRED]
> Unhinged Development Group Ltd, registered in Scotland (SC number), registered office address. Contact email for data queries.

**Data Protection Officer** [REQUIRED — if you have one; flag if you don't]
> A DPO is required where processing is "large scale" or "systematic" monitoring — at Groomr's current headcount, you almost certainly do not need one. But state in the notice whether one is appointed or not, and provide a contact point for data queries regardless.

**What data we collect and hold** [REQUIRED]
> Categories:
> - Identity data: name, job title, date of birth, national insurance number, passport/right-to-work documents.
> - Contact data: address, phone, email.
> - Financial data: bank account, salary, payroll records, pension details, expense claims, P60/P11D.
> - Health/special-category data: sickness records, occupational health reports, disability information, pregnancy.
> - Performance data: appraisal records, disciplinary and grievance records, training records.
> - Device and system data: email, system access logs, device-use records (if monitored).
> - Recruitment data: application, CV, interview notes, references.

**Lawful basis for each category** [REQUIRED]
> - Most employment processing: **performance of a contract** (processing necessary to perform or prepare to enter an employment contract) or **legal obligation** (payroll, right-to-work checks, HMRC compliance, auto-enrolment).
> - Health/special-category data: **employment law obligation** (sickness, statutory pay calculations, reasonable adjustments) under DPA 2018 Schedule 1 para 1.
> - Monitoring (if applicable): **legitimate interests** — must be weighed against employee rights and documented in a DPIA.
> - Disciplinary/grievance records: **legitimate interests** (maintaining fair processes, defending legal claims).

**How long we keep it** [REQUIRED]
> A retention schedule by data type is the right approach. Indicative periods:
> - Payroll/HMRC records: 6 years (HMRC requirement).
> - Right-to-work documents: 2 years post-employment termination.
> - Disciplinary/grievance records: duration of employment + 1–2 years (depending on whether a claim could still be brought; note the 3-month primary ET limitation period, with discretion to extend).
> - Sickness/health records: duration of employment + a retention period consistent with potential employment claims. Health data needs particular care — minimise retention.
> - Recruitment records for unsuccessful candidates: typically 6 months (long enough to defend a discrimination claim at ET; discrimination claims have a 3-month primary period).

**Who we share it with** [REQUIRED]
> HMRC, The Pensions Regulator / pension scheme provider, Clerk (authentication data if any employee uses the platform), Supabase (if employee data processed there), payroll bureau (if used), HR software providers, external solicitors (if a claim is threatened or brought), occupational health providers, insurance company.

**International transfers** [REQUIRED — check each processor]
> Verify whether any processors (Supabase, Clerk, Cloudinary) transfer data outside the UK. Each needs either UK adequacy, standard contractual clauses, or another transfer mechanism. This is a live compliance obligation, not a hypothetical.

**Data subject rights** [REQUIRED]
> Right to access (DSAR); right to rectification; right to erasure (limited in employment — much data is retained for legal compliance reasons); right to restriction; right to portability (structured, machine-readable format on request); right to object to legitimate-interests processing. Right to complain to the ICO. Response time: 1 calendar month (extendable by 2 further months for complex requests).

**Automated decision-making** [REQUIRED — state if used or not]
> State whether any decisions with significant legal or similar effect are made by automated means (e.g. automated performance scoring, AI-driven recruitment screening). If yes: explain the logic, significance, and likely consequences, and state the right to request human review.

**Monitoring** [REQUIRED — if applicable; see also Remote Working Policy]
> If you monitor device use, emails, internet activity, or location: state this here, along with the lawful basis and the DPIA reference. Without this disclosure, monitoring is unlawful under UK GDPR and potentially RIPA 2000.

### 4. Marketplace-specific considerations
Employee data and platform user data (dog owners, groomers) must be kept in entirely separate processing streams. There should be no overlap between the employee data register and the platform user database. Make this explicit in your data register (Record of Processing Activities — ROPA — required under UK GDPR Article 30 for organisations processing data "on a large scale", but good practice regardless).

### 5. Animal-welfare considerations
Not applicable.

### 6. Cross-references
- Remote Working Policy (monitoring section)
- Use of Company Equipment Policy (device monitoring)
- Disciplinary & Grievance Policy (processing of disciplinary records)
- Sickness Policy (processing of health data)
- Whistleblowing Policy (confidentiality of disclosures — handled under separate legal basis)

### 7. Risk flags — get a solicitor on these
- **International transfers to US processors (Supabase, Clerk, Cloudinary):** post-Schrems II, the legal basis for UK-to-US transfers needs to be confirmed for each processor — UK-US Data Bridge (adequacy) covers some organisations; for others you need standard contractual clauses in the DPA with the processor. This is an active compliance obligation.
- **Health data (special category):** the Schedule 1 condition (DPA 2018) must be identified and documented in your ROPA for every health-data processing activity. Get a DPA/GDPR specialist to review this section — the ICO has fined employers specifically for mishandling employee health data.

### 8. Decisions needed from you
- Payroll software: in-house or bureau? (Determines who the processor is.)
- HR software: any HRIS being used or planned? (Adds a processor.)
- Monitoring: what, if anything, is monitored on company devices?
- DPO: will you appoint one proactively (not required at current scale, but some investors expect it)?

## Policy 5: Parental Leave Policy

### 1. Purpose & scope
- Consolidates all statutory parental leave entitlements in one place, and states whether and how the company enhances them.
- Covers: maternity leave and pay, paternity leave and pay, adoption leave and pay, shared parental leave and pay, parental leave (unpaid), and parental bereavement leave.
- Applies to all employees. Some rights also apply to "workers" — note where relevant.
- One of the most frequently misunderstood areas of employment law — employees often do not know their rights; managers often do not know their obligations. A clear, plain-English policy saves an enormous amount of difficulty.

### 2. Regulatory drivers
- **Employment Rights Act 1996 + Maternity and Paternity Leave Regs 1999 [REQUIRED]:** maternity leave entitlement (up to 52 weeks), right to return to the same or equivalent role, keeping-in-touch (KIT) days.
- **Statutory Maternity Pay Regs 1986 [REQUIRED]:** SMP eligibility (26 weeks' service by the qualifying week), rates (currently 90% of average weekly earnings for first 6 weeks, then flat rate — verify current rate), duration (39 weeks).
- **Paternity Leave Regs 2002 [REQUIRED]:** 1–2 weeks' ordinary paternity leave; currently being reviewed — the *Paternity Leave (Bereavement) Act 2024* and government proposals to extend paternity leave are in progress; verify current position.
- **Shared Parental Leave Regs 2014 [REQUIRED]:** eligible parents can share up to 50 weeks' leave (up to 37 weeks paid). Complex to administer — the policy must explain the notice process clearly.
- **Parental Leave Regs 1999 [REQUIRED]:** unpaid parental leave — 18 weeks per child for employees with 1 year's service, up to the child's 18th birthday, maximum 4 weeks per year per child. Must be documented.
- **Parental Bereavement Leave and Pay Act 2020 (Jack's Law) [REQUIRED]:** statutory right to 2 weeks' paid leave for any employee who loses a child under 18 or suffers a stillbirth from 24 weeks. Day-one right. Statutory Parental Bereavement Pay for eligible employees.
- **Adoption Leave Regs 2002 (as amended) [REQUIRED]:** equivalent rights to maternity for the primary adopter; secondary adopter gets paternity leave equivalent.
- **Equality Act 2010 [REQUIRED]:** the "protected period" during pregnancy and maternity leave. Treating an employee unfavourably during this period is automatically discriminatory without requiring a comparator. The period begins at the start of pregnancy and ends when the employee returns from maternity leave.

### 3. Recommended sections

**Maternity leave and pay** [REQUIRED]
> Entitlement (52 weeks: 26 ordinary + 26 additional). SMP eligibility. Enhanced maternity pay (if any — see decisions section). Notice requirements (by the 15th week before the expected week of childbirth). KIT days (up to 10, optional, paid at normal rate). Right to return. Redundancy protections during leave. Health and safety for pregnant employees (risk assessment duty).

**Paternity leave and pay** [REQUIRED]
> 1–2 weeks' ordinary paternity leave; entitlement date (birth or placement). Notice requirements. Statutory Paternity Pay (SPP) rate. Enhanced pay (if any). Note: flag that paternity leave reform is an active area of legislation and verify the current position.

**Adoption leave and pay** [REQUIRED]
> Primary adopter: mirrors maternity. Secondary adopter: mirrors paternity. Notify employer within 7 days of being matched. Foster-for-adoption: flag the position is complex; the right applies from placement. Overseas adoption: different rules — get HR/legal advice on a case-by-case basis.

**Shared Parental Leave (SPL)** [REQUIRED — but acknowledge the complexity]
> State the principle clearly: eligible parents can curtail maternity/adoption leave and share up to 50 weeks' leave (37 paid) between them, taken in blocks. The notice process is genuinely complex (curtailment notices, period-of-leave notices, the 14-day response window). Provide a worked example or flowchart — this is one of the most misunderstood entitlements. Enhanced pay (if any) must be matched between eligible parents to avoid sex-discrimination exposure (see risk flags).

**Parental leave (unpaid)** [REQUIRED]
> 18 weeks per child, up to child's 18th birthday, max 4 weeks per year per child; 1 year's service required; 21 days' notice for a block; employer can postpone (not refuse) up to 6 months where business disruption is significant. Rarely used but must be documented.

**Parental bereavement leave** [REQUIRED — Jack's Law]
> 2 weeks for loss of a child under 18 or stillbirth from 24 weeks. Day-one right. No notice formality required during the acute period. Statutory Parental Bereavement Pay (SPBP) for eligible employees. Handle with sensitivity — a policy statement here should be brief and compassionate in tone.

**Keeping in Touch (KIT) days** [BEST PRACTICE]
> Up to 10 paid KIT days during maternity/adoption leave (SPL has 20 SPLIT days). Mutual agreement required. Do not use KIT days as a soft pressure mechanism — state clearly they are entirely voluntary.

**Returning from leave** [REQUIRED]
> Right to return to the same role (after ordinary maternity/adoption leave) or equivalent role (after additional leave). State what "equivalent" means in practice. Flexible working request on return: flag the right (and cross-reference the Flexible Working Policy).

**Enhanced pay (above statutory)** [COMMERCIAL CHOICE — significant cost decision]
> Whether you pay above SMP/SPP during any period. Many early-stage companies offer enhanced maternity for the first X weeks and/or an enhanced paternity offer to signal culture. If you offer enhanced maternity but not enhanced shared parental pay, you are exposed to an indirect sex discrimination argument (the Snell v Network Rail line of cases). Get a solicitor to confirm the approach.

### 4. Marketplace-specific considerations
Groomers on the platform are not employees and have no entitlement to statutory parental leave. Misclassification risk: if a groomer could argue worker status, they would have access to certain parental pay entitlements. This is a platform/commercial risk, not an HR risk — but flag it.

### 5. Animal-welfare considerations
Not applicable.

### 6. Cross-references
- Equal Opportunities & Anti-Discrimination Policy (Equality Act protected period; equal treatment)
- Flexible Working Policy (requests on return from leave)
- Employee Benefits Policy (enhanced pay sits here as a benefit)
- Holidays Policy (holiday accrues during all parental leave)
- Sickness Policy (if sickness coincides with maternity/adoption leave — complex interaction)

### 7. Risk flags — get a solicitor on these
- **Enhanced shared parental pay vs. enhanced maternity pay:** if you offer enhanced maternity pay but only statutory SPP/ShPP to eligible fathers/partners, this creates a direct comparator argument. The safe options are: (a) pay the same enhancement regardless of which leave type the parent takes, or (b) have a solicitor advise on your specific enhancement structure before it's published.
- **Redundancy during maternity/adoption leave:** employees on maternity/adoption leave have a statutory right to be offered any suitable alternative vacancy before other at-risk employees. Failure to comply is automatically unfair dismissal. Make sure this is in your redundancy procedure.

### 8. Decisions needed from you
- Will you offer enhanced maternity pay above SMP? If yes: at what rate and for how long?
- Will you offer enhanced paternity pay above SPP?
- Will you match maternity enhancement for SPL-eligible partners?
- What is the qualifying service period for any enhancements (day one, or after probation)?
- Will there be a return-to-work condition attached to enhancements (e.g. return for at least 3 months or repay the enhancement — get legal advice on the enforceability of this)?

## Policy 6: Whistleblowing Policy

### 1. Purpose & scope
- Governs the process by which employees can raise concerns about wrongdoing in the workplace — legal violations, health and safety dangers, miscarriages of justice, environmental damage, or the deliberate concealment of any of the above.
- Applies to all employees and "workers" (including contractors who personally perform work — broader category than "employee").
- Distinct from the Grievance Procedure: a grievance is a personal employment complaint; whistleblowing (a protected disclosure) is a concern about something that is in the public interest, not merely of personal concern. The distinction matters because the legal protections are very different.

### 2. Regulatory drivers
- **Public Interest Disclosure Act 1998 (PIDA) [REQUIRED]:** employees and workers who make a "qualifying disclosure" are protected from detriment and dismissal. The disclosure must be in the reasonable belief of the worker and must be in the public interest (not purely personal). Protected disclosures can be made internally, to a prescribed person, or (in limited circumstances) more widely.
- **Employment Rights Act 1996 Part IVA [REQUIRED]:** the statutory protections — detriment (s.47B) and dismissal (s.103A). Dismissal for a protected disclosure is automatically unfair and has no qualifying service period — day-one protection.
- **Financial Services whistleblowing rules [FLAG — verify applicability]:** FCA/PRA rules require certain financial services firms to have a whistleblowing champion and formal channels. Groomr is not FCA-regulated at present (Stripe carries the payment regulation), but if you take in-house payment processing or e-money functions in future, this becomes relevant.
- **Criminal Finances Act 2017 [BEST PRACTICE]:** the failure-to-prevent facilitation-of-tax-evasion offence creates an incentive to have robust reporting channels.

### 3. Recommended sections

**What qualifies as a protected disclosure** [REQUIRED]
> State the six categories from PIDA: (1) criminal offences; (2) failure to comply with a legal obligation; (3) miscarriages of justice; (4) danger to health or safety; (5) damage to the environment; (6) deliberate concealment of any of the above. Use plain language, not statutory language. Examples help: "if you believe the company is committing fraud, breaching data protection law, or endangering someone's health or safety, this is the process."

**What does NOT qualify (and should use other channels)** [REQUIRED]
> Personal employment grievances (use the Grievance Procedure); commercial dissatisfaction; policy disagreements. Make the distinction clear — a common error is employees treating personal complaints as whistleblowing to obtain PIDA protection, and managers treating genuine whistleblowing as merely a grievance.

**How to make a disclosure — internal channels** [REQUIRED]
> Who to report to: line manager, or senior management if the line manager is implicated, or a designated whistleblowing officer (which could be the founder at this stage). State that disclosures should be in writing where possible (to establish the record), but can be verbal. The company must acknowledge receipt and confirm it will investigate.

**Prescribed persons — external channels** [REQUIRED]
> PIDA protects disclosures to a prescribed person (a regulator relevant to the concern) even if the internal process has not been exhausted. List the most relevant prescribed persons for Groomr: ICO (data protection concerns), HMRC (tax evasion), Health and Safety Executive, Financial Conduct Authority (if relevant), Companies House. The full list is maintained by BEIS — reference it.

**Wider disclosure** [REQUIRED — explain the narrow conditions]
> Disclosure to the press or wider public is protected only in very limited circumstances (previous internal disclosure not acted on, belief of cover-up, etc.) and only where the other conditions in PIDA are met. State that employees should seek legal advice before making a wider disclosure — not to discourage it, but because the legal conditions are exacting.

**Confidentiality** [REQUIRED]
> The company will treat disclosures confidentially and will not reveal the identity of the person who raised the concern without their consent — except where legally required (e.g. the information must be disclosed to police or a regulator). State this explicitly and deliver on it — confidentiality failures are a common detriment claim.

**Investigation process** [BEST PRACTICE]
> Who investigates (independent of the subject of the concern); what the timeline looks like; whether an outcome is communicated to the discloser and in what form; how records are kept. The discloser is not entitled to a full investigation report (third-party confidentiality, ongoing proceedings), but they should receive a summary outcome.

**Protection from detriment and retaliation** [REQUIRED]
> State that retaliation against a whistleblower is prohibited and will be treated as gross misconduct. Retaliation includes: dismissal, demotion, exclusion, being denied promotion, hostile treatment by colleagues. Detriment claims under PIDA can be brought at Employment Tribunal; there is no qualifying service period and no cap on compensation.

**Anonymous disclosures** [COMMERCIAL CHOICE]
> Whether anonymous disclosures are accepted. The practical issue: anonymous disclosures are harder to investigate and cannot always result in a finding. State whether you accept them, that you will investigate as far as possible, but that you may not be able to update the discloser.

### 4. Marketplace-specific considerations
Groomers on the platform are not employees and are not covered by PIDA. However, Groomr has a general interest in groomers being able to flag concerns about platform conduct — a platform-level reporting channel (separate from this employment policy) is good practice and may become a regulatory expectation under the Online Safety Act. Keep the two channels entirely separate.

### 5. Animal-welfare considerations
Animal welfare concerns could qualify as a protected disclosure under PIDA if they involve a breach of legal obligation (e.g. an employee becoming aware that groomers are systematically breaching Animal Welfare Act obligations and the company is covering it up). Unlikely but note the potential.

### 6. Cross-references
- Disciplinary & Grievance Policy (distinct procedures — make the boundary explicit)
- Anti-Harassment & Bullying Policy (protection from retaliation for whistleblowers)
- Employee Privacy Notice (processing of whistleblowing records — separate legal basis)

### 7. Risk flags — get a solicitor on these
- **Automatic unfair dismissal for protected disclosure has no service threshold** — an employee can be dismissed for whistleblowing on day one of employment and bring an automatically unfair dismissal claim without limit on compensation. This is one of the highest-value, hardest-to-defend claims at Employment Tribunal. Get the policy right.
- **Records of disclosures** are special-category-adjacent — they often reveal the identity of the discloser and the subject. Store them separately with restricted access. Consider whether a DPIA is needed.

### 8. Decisions needed from you
- Will you appoint a named whistleblowing officer (separate from the line management chain)?
- External channel: will you use a third-party whistleblowing hotline (relatively cheap and adds credibility to the confidentiality commitment)?
- Will you accept anonymous disclosures?

## Policy 7: Health & Safety Policy

### 1. Purpose & scope
- Governs the company's approach to ensuring the health, safety, and welfare of employees and others affected by company activities.
- Required in writing for employers with 5 or more employees. Write it before you reach that threshold — retrofitting it under pressure is messy.
- Three parts (statutory structure): a statement of intent (general H&S commitment), an organisation section (who is responsible for what), and an arrangements section (specific procedures).
- For Groomr at this stage: predominantly an office/remote-working context. The main H&S risks are DSE-related (screen work), lone working (if employees work remotely or off-site), fire, and manual handling (minimal). The risk profile is low relative to industrial employers, but the legal obligations are identical.

### 2. Regulatory drivers
- **Health and Safety at Work Act 1974 [REQUIRED]:** general duty to ensure, so far as reasonably practicable, the health, safety, and welfare of employees. Also a duty to persons not in employment (visitors, contractors, delivery personnel at any office). Includes the duty to provide safe systems of work, safe equipment, training, and supervision.
- **Management of Health and Safety at Work Regs 1999 [REQUIRED]:** written risk assessments for all significant hazards; appoint a competent person for H&S; implement emergency procedures; provide H&S information and training.
- **Display Screen Equipment (DSE) Regs 1992 [REQUIRED]:** assessment required for every employee who habitually uses a screen (workstation assessment); provision of spectacles if required for screen work. Applies to home workstations for remote workers — see Remote Working Policy.
- **Workplace (Health, Safety and Welfare) Regs 1992 [REQUIRED — for any physical office space]:** temperature, lighting, ventilation, sanitation, rest facilities minimum standards.
- **Employer's Liability (Compulsory Insurance) Act 1969 [REQUIRED]:** EL insurance must be displayed (or accessible online). Certificate of insurance must be accessible to employees.
- **Reporting of Injuries, Diseases and Dangerous Occurrences Regs 2013 (RIDDOR) [REQUIRED]:** reportable incidents (deaths, specified injuries, over-7-day incapacitation, occupational diseases, dangerous occurrences) must be reported to the HSE.
- **Health and Safety (First Aid) Regs 1981 [REQUIRED]:** at minimum, a stocked first-aid box and a nominated person to take charge in an emergency (not necessarily a trained first-aider at very small headcount — verify with HSE guidance). Appoint a trained first-aider once you exceed a threshold appropriate to your risk assessment.
- **Mental Health [BEST PRACTICE — regulatory direction of travel]:** no standalone statute yet for employer mental health obligations, but HSE's stress management standards (and the Management Regs' requirement to assess all risks including psychological risks) mean a documented approach to work-related stress is expected. The Health and Safety Executive has enforcement tools for employers ignoring psychological safety.

### 3. Recommended sections

**Statement of intent** [REQUIRED]
> Signed by the most senior person. Commits the company to providing a safe workplace, meeting legal obligations, consulting employees on H&S matters, and reviewing the policy regularly. Brief — half a page maximum.

**Responsibilities — organisation** [REQUIRED]
> Who is responsible for each H&S function: the employer (overall), any designated H&S officer (even if that's the founder at this stage), all employees (cooperation duty under HASAWA s.7). At small headcount, keep it simple: name the competent person.

**Risk assessments** [REQUIRED]
> What hazards have been assessed; findings; control measures. At office level: fire, electrical safety, DSE, lone working, first aid, slips/trips. For remote workers: DSE self-assessment. Review whenever work activities change.

**DSE workstation assessments** [REQUIRED]
> Process for new starters; process for home workers (self-assessment form); action on identified issues (equipment provision, spectacle contribution). Cross-reference Remote Working Policy.

**Fire safety** [REQUIRED]
> Emergency evacuation plan; fire risk assessment (landlord may hold this for shared office, but verify); fire warden(s) designated; testing and drill schedule; employee briefing on joining.

**First aid** [REQUIRED]
> Location of first-aid kit; named first-aid point person; procedure for accidents; RIDDOR reporting trigger.

**Accident and incident reporting** [REQUIRED]
> How employees report accidents/near-misses; incident log; RIDDOR reporting threshold and process; investigation of incidents.

**Work-related stress and mental health** [BEST PRACTICE]
> Reference the HSE Management Standards for work-related stress (demands, control, support, relationships, role, change). Commit to assessing psychosocial risks as part of the risk-assessment programme. Reference the EAP.

**Lone working** [REQUIRED if applicable]
> For remote workers or any employee working off-site: check-in procedure, emergency contact, what to do if contact is missed.

**Contractor and visitor safety** [REQUIRED]
> Brief contractors entering any company premises or working under company supervision on relevant H&S rules.

**Policy review** [REQUIRED]
> State the review frequency (annually, or following any significant accident, change in activities, or change in legislation).

### 4. Marketplace-specific considerations
Groomers are not employees — Groomr has no H&S obligation to groomers as a platform operator in the way an employer does. However: if Groomr ever directs groomer activities closely enough to constitute control over the manner of their work, it could acquire H&S duties. This is the same misclassification landmine discussed elsewhere — maintain the platform's arm's-length character.

### 5. Animal-welfare considerations
Not applicable to this policy (H&S covers humans; animal welfare is governed by the Animal Welfare Acts and Groomr's platform policies).

### 6. Cross-references
- Remote Working Policy (DSE assessment for home workers)
- Sickness Policy (work-related ill health, return-to-work)
- Anti-Harassment & Bullying Policy (psychological safety and H&S overlap)
- Employee Benefits Policy (EAP as mental health support)

### 7. Risk flags — get a solicitor on these
- **Psychological risk assessments** are increasingly expected by the HSE and are the legal basis for stress-related personal injury claims (arising from breach of the Management Regs duty). Document them even informally at this stage.
- **Remote worker H&S:** the duty of care extends to home workspaces. You cannot inspect an employee's home, but you can require completion of a DSE self-assessment and act on red flags (e.g. providing equipment). Document what you asked and what you provided.

### 8. Decisions needed from you
- Physical office: will you have one, or fully remote? (Fire safety, workstation, and welfare obligations differ.)
- Will you appoint a trained first-aider from day one or rely on a nominated point person initially?
- Who is the named H&S competent person at this stage — internal hire or external H&S consultant?

## Policy 8: Probation Policy

### 1. Purpose & scope
- Governs the probationary period at the start of employment: its purpose, duration, the review process, what happens when performance is unsatisfactory, and how probation ends (pass or dismissal).
- Applies to all new employees unless the employment contract expressly states no probationary period applies.
- Probation is frequently misunderstood — it does not give the employer a free pass to dismiss without process. It provides a shorter, less procedurally intensive path to dismissal, but only within the 2-year unfair-dismissal qualifying period. Several day-one rights (automatic unfair dismissal for whistleblowing, discrimination, asserting a statutory right) mean you can never simply dismiss without any reason.

### 2. Regulatory drivers
- **Employment Rights Act 1996 [REQUIRED]:** unfair dismissal protection applies after 2 years' service. A probationary dismissal within 2 years does not need to meet the full "fair reason + fair procedure" test — but the employer must still act reasonably and not dismiss for a prohibited reason.
- **Equality Act 2010 [REQUIRED]:** dismissal during probation for a reason related to a protected characteristic is automatically discriminatory (day-one right). This includes dismissal connected to pregnancy (from the moment the employer knows of it), disability, or race.
- **ACAS Code of Practice [BEST PRACTICE — even for probationary dismissal]:** while not strictly required for sub-2-year dismissals, following a basic process (discussion of concerns, opportunity to improve, warning before dismissal) is strongly recommended. Tribunals can still hear discrimination and automatically unfair dismissal claims regardless of service length, and poor process looks bad.
- **Working Time Regulations 1998 [REQUIRED]:** WTR rights (holiday, rest breaks) apply from day one — not after probation.

### 3. Recommended sections

**Probation period length** [COMMERCIAL CHOICE]
> Typically 3–6 months for most roles; up to 12 months for senior hires. State the standard period in the policy and any role-specific variants in the employment contract. Note: the probation period is part of continuous employment for statutory purposes — it counts toward the 2-year unfair-dismissal qualifying period.

**Purpose of probation** [BEST PRACTICE]
> State clearly: probation is a structured period to assess suitability for the role and the company, and to provide support and feedback. Not a trial period during which the employer can dismiss for any reason — be honest about this internally to avoid managerial overconfidence.

**Induction and support during probation** [BEST PRACTICE]
> New employees should be given adequate induction (systems access, culture, role expectations). State what's provided and by when. A failure to induct properly and then dismissing for "not meeting expectations" is legally risky and operationally poor.

**Probation reviews** [REQUIRED in practical terms]
> Minimum: a mid-point review and an end-of-probation review. Document both, even if informal. Cover: what is going well, what needs to improve, any additional support or training needed, and whether the employee is on track to pass. If concerns are identified, say so at the mid-point — not only at the end.

**Unsatisfactory performance or conduct during probation** [REQUIRED]
> If performance or conduct is unsatisfactory, the employee must be told specifically: what the concern is, what improvement is required, by when, and what support will be provided. A "probation extension" (typically 4–8 weeks) is the usual tool — state the maximum extension period (most employers limit to one extension). Document the conversation.

**Dismissal during probation** [REQUIRED]
> State that unsatisfactory performance or conduct that does not improve following feedback may result in dismissal before or at the end of the probation period. Dismissal requires notice (or PILON if that clause is in the contract). Written confirmation of dismissal with reasons. Right of appeal (even during probation — ACAS Code encourages this). Do not cite a prohibited reason (pregnancy, whistleblowing, etc.) — even with no intent, if those facts are present, the claim risk is high.

**Successful completion** [BEST PRACTICE]
> Confirm in writing that probation has been passed. Note: probation completion does not change any of the statutory rights — it is a contractual milestone, not a legal status change.

**Absence and health conditions during probation** [REQUIRED — care needed]
> Extended sickness during probation extends the meaningful assessment period, but dismissing for sickness absence that amounts to a disability is unlawful under the Equality Act regardless of service length. If a health condition emerges during probation, seek HR/legal advice before taking any action.

### 4. Marketplace-specific considerations
Not applicable — this is a pure employment policy.

### 5. Animal-welfare considerations
Not applicable.

### 6. Cross-references
- Disciplinary & Grievance Policy (conduct concerns during probation may follow a lighter version of the same process)
- Equal Opportunities & Anti-Discrimination Policy (day-one discrimination protections apply in full during probation)
- Sickness Policy (interaction with health-related absence during probation)

### 7. Risk flags — get a solicitor on these
- **Pregnancy discovered during probation** — dismissal of a pregnant employee (or one who has recently disclosed pregnancy) during probation, even with a performance-based reason, carries an extremely high discrimination claim risk. The burden of proof effectively shifts to the employer to show the reason was entirely unrelated to the pregnancy. Take legal advice immediately if this situation arises.
- **Extending probation beyond the employment contract's stated period** — if the contract states a 6-month probation, you cannot unilaterally extend to 12 months without a contractual variation agreed with the employee.

### 8. Decisions needed from you
- Standard probation period: 3 months, 6 months?
- Extension: one extension permitted, maximum duration?
- Right of appeal against probationary dismissal: yes (recommended) or no?

## Policy 9: Flexible Working Policy

### 1. Purpose & scope
- Governs the statutory right to request flexible working arrangements, the process for making and responding to requests, and the basis on which requests may be refused.
- Applies to all employees from day one of employment (following the Employment Relations (Flexible Working) Act 2023, in force April 2024).
- Distinct from the Remote Working Policy (which governs how people work remotely once an arrangement is in place) — this policy governs how flexible arrangements are agreed.

### 2. Regulatory drivers
- **Employment Relations (Flexible Working) Act 2023 + Employment Rights Act 1996 (as amended) [REQUIRED]:** key changes from April 2024: right to request from day one (previously 26 weeks); up to two requests per year (previously one); employer must respond within two months (previously three); employer must consult the employee before rejecting a request; no requirement on the employee to explain the impact of the change on the business.
- **Equality Act 2010 [REQUIRED]:** refusing flexible working requests at a higher rate for employees with certain protected characteristics (e.g. women with childcare responsibilities, disabled employees, employees with religious observance needs) can constitute indirect discrimination. Document all refusals and the objective justification.
- **Working Time Regulations 1998 [REQUIRED]:** flexible arrangements must not result in employees being unable to take statutory rest breaks and minimum rest periods.
- **Part-Time Workers Regulations 2000 [REQUIRED]:** if a flexible working request results in a move to part-time hours, the employee's terms must not be less favourable on a pro-rata basis without objective justification.

### 3. Recommended sections

**Who can apply and when** [REQUIRED]
> All employees from day one. No minimum service requirement. Clearly distinguish this right from informal agreements — informal flexibility given at manager discretion does not become a formal flexible working arrangement under this policy unless documented as such.

**Types of flexible working** [BEST PRACTICE — illustrative list]
> Changes to hours (part-time, compressed week, reduced hours); changes to start/finish times; job sharing; term-time only working; annualised hours; remote/home working as a formal arrangement (this is also a flexible working request — link to Remote Working Policy).

**How to make a request** [REQUIRED]
> In writing; state the proposed arrangement, the date it would start, the employee's assessment of the effect on the business, and how that effect might be dealt with. The 2023 Act removed the requirement to explain the effect — but employees still benefit from doing so as it makes the request easier to grant.

**How the company will respond** [REQUIRED]
> Must consult with the employee (meeting or written) before refusing. Decision within 2 months (from request to appeal outcome, if an appeal is submitted). Written decision with reasons. If granting: confirm the new arrangement in writing (and note whether it is a permanent contractual change or a trial).

**Grounds for refusal** [REQUIRED — eight statutory grounds only]
> Refusal is only lawful on one or more of these eight grounds: (1) burden of additional costs; (2) detrimental effect on ability to meet customer demand; (3) inability to reorganise work among existing staff; (4) inability to recruit additional staff; (5) detrimental impact on quality; (6) detrimental impact on performance; (7) insufficiency of work during the periods the employee proposes to work; (8) planned structural changes. Document which ground(s) apply — a refusal without grounds is automatically unlawful.

**Trial periods** [COMMERCIAL CHOICE]
> Whether you offer a trial period (typically 3–6 months) before making a flexible arrangement permanent. This is not a statutory concept but is practically useful — state whether you use them and how they work.

**Permanent contractual change** [REQUIRED — important practical point]
> Agreed flexible arrangements become permanent contractual changes unless explicitly stated as temporary. A trial or temporary arrangement must be documented as such, or the employee can rely on it as a permanent term of employment. Get any temporary arrangement in writing with a clear end date and review mechanism.

**Appeal** [BEST PRACTICE — ACAS Code recommends it]
> State the right of appeal against a refusal, the timeframe, and who hears it.

### 4. Marketplace-specific considerations
Not applicable — pure employment policy.

### 5. Animal-welfare considerations
Not applicable.

### 6. Cross-references
- Remote Working Policy (the operational framework for remote arrangements)
- Parental Leave Policy (flexible working requests on return from maternity/parental leave are common)
- Equal Opportunities & Anti-Discrimination Policy (refusal patterns)

### 7. Risk flags — get a solicitor on these
- **Indirect discrimination** from patterns of refusal — if flexible working requests are refused more often from women, carers, or disabled employees, that is indirect discrimination exposure even if each individual refusal was technically on a valid ground. Keep records and review patterns.
- **Temporary vs. permanent** — the single most common practical error. Every manager who "agrees to see how it goes for a bit" without documentation may have created a permanent contractual variation.

### 8. Decisions needed from you
- Will you offer trial periods? If yes, how long?
- Will you have a standard form for flexible working requests, or accept free-form written requests?
- Who is the appeal decision-maker if the company is small?

## Policy 10: Social Media & Communications Policy

### 1. Purpose & scope
- Governs employee use of social media — both on company accounts and personal accounts in work-related contexts.
- Also covers broader digital communications: messaging apps, work email, instant messaging platforms (Slack), and public forums.
- Applies to all employees, in and out of working hours where professional conduct or company reputation is implicated.
- For Groomr specifically, this policy carries higher-than-average importance: the brand operates in a consumer-trust industry, and employee conduct on platforms like LinkedIn, Instagram, and TikTok directly reflects on brand perception in the Scottish/UK pet-care community.

### 2. Regulatory drivers
- **Online Safety Act 2023 [REQUIRED awareness]:** while this Act primarily governs platforms, employees generating and distributing illegal content via company platforms or channels create direct company liability.
- **Defamation Act 2013 (E&W) / Defamation and Malicious Publication (Scotland) Act 2021 [REQUIRED awareness]:** posts that are false and damage the reputation of an identifiable person or business create liability — for the author and potentially the employer if made in a work context. Scotland's 2021 Act modernised defamation law differently from England's 2013 Act — note the divergence.
- **UK GDPR + DPA 2018 [REQUIRED]:** sharing personal data about colleagues, users, or groomers on social media is a data breach. Employees must be trained on this — the "personal use" exception does not apply to sharing work-related personal data.
- **Equality Act 2010 [REQUIRED]:** posting content that harasses a colleague based on a protected characteristic, even on a personal account, can create employer liability if the employer failed to take reasonable steps to prevent it (especially under the Worker Protection Act 2023).
- **Computer Misuse Act 1990 [REQUIRED awareness]:** unauthorised access to others' social media accounts (e.g. sharing login credentials for company accounts without authorisation) is a criminal offence.

### 3. Recommended sections

**Company social media accounts** [REQUIRED]
> Who has access, who is authorised to post, who approves content, what the approval process is. Password management and handover on employment termination. Prohibition on personal opinions being posted through company accounts.

**Personal social media — the ground rules** [REQUIRED]
> Employees retain the right to personal social media use. However, where a post references the company, the industry, colleagues, or clients — directly or identifiably — the policy applies. Key rules:
> - Do not post confidential company information.
> - Do not post personal data about colleagues, users, or groomers.
> - Do not make disparaging statements about the company, its clients, competitors, or colleagues.
> - Do not hold yourself out as an official company spokesperson unless authorised.
> - Do not post content that would violate the Equal Opportunities, Anti-Harassment, or any other company policy.

**LinkedIn and professional profiles** [COMMERCIAL CHOICE]
> Whether employees are expected to list Groomr in their professional profile (and in what terms). Recommended for employer brand; cannot be required, but encourage it. If encouraged, provide approved language for "about" sections.

**Monitoring of personal social media** [REQUIRED — strict rules apply]
> State whether and how the company monitors employee social media activity. Monitoring of personal accounts requires a lawful basis under UK GDPR, and covert monitoring is almost never justified. Transparency is required. A DPIA must be documented if any monitoring tool is in use.

**Work communications (email, Slack, messaging)** [REQUIRED]
> Work communications are monitored only in accordance with the Employee Privacy Notice. Email and Slack are company systems — there is no reasonable expectation of privacy on work accounts. State this clearly so it is not a surprise.

**Public commentary on the industry** [COMMERCIAL CHOICE]
> Whether employees with relevant expertise are encouraged or discouraged from public commentary (e.g. LinkedIn posts about the pet-care industry). For a startup in a specialist field, employee thought leadership can be valuable — if you want to encourage it, state so and provide light-touch guidelines.

**Consequences of breach** [REQUIRED]
> Violation of this policy may constitute misconduct (Disciplinary Procedure). Severity depends on the nature and reach of the violation — sharing confidential data publicly vs. a personal opinion that doesn't identify the company are different in degree.

### 4. Marketplace-specific considerations
Groomers are not employees — this policy does not apply to them. However, there is a platform-level interest in how groomers represent their connection to Groomr on social media (endorsements, complaints, misleading claims). The Groomer Terms of Service is where that is governed.

### 5. Animal-welfare considerations
Not applicable directly. If an employee posts content that suggests animal welfare failures in connection with groomers on the platform, this is a Whistleblowing Policy matter as well as a social media policy matter — route appropriately.

### 6. Cross-references
- Use of Company Equipment Policy (use of company devices for personal social media)
- Confidentiality & IP Assignment Policy (what constitutes confidential information)
- Anti-Harassment & Bullying Policy (online harassment between colleagues)
- Employee Privacy Notice (monitoring of communications)
- Disciplinary & Grievance Policy (enforcement)

### 7. Risk flags — get a solicitor on these
- **Monitoring personal social media accounts:** this is a high-risk area. Even reactive monitoring (searching for employees' names after an incident) must be justified and documented. Covert monitoring of personal accounts is very hard to justify under UK GDPR and ICO guidance.
- **Gross misconduct threshold for social media:** courts and tribunals have established a nuanced case law on whether personal social media posts justify summary dismissal. The key factors: how identifiable the company is, whether the post caused actual damage, and whether the company's policy was clear and had been communicated. Get the policy in front of employees with a signed acknowledgement.

### 8. Decisions needed from you
- Will you actively monitor company-related social media mentions?
- Will you encourage employee thought leadership on social/professional platforms? If yes, with what guardrails?
- Company social media: who holds the master account credentials and how are they held securely?

## Policy 11: Confidentiality & IP Assignment Policy

### 1. Purpose & scope
- Governs what information employees must keep confidential (during and after employment) and who owns the intellectual property created in the course of employment.
- Applies to all employees from day one. The core obligations (especially post-termination confidentiality and IP assignment) must be in the employment contract — the policy supplements and explains those contractual clauses.
- Particularly important for Groomr as a technology company: code, product features, design, algorithms, platform architecture, user data, commercial relationships, and business strategy are all company IP or confidential information. An employee departing with any of this to a competitor is an existential risk.

### 2. Regulatory drivers
- **Copyright, Designs and Patents Act 1988 (CDPA) s.11 [REQUIRED]:** copyright in works created by an employee in the course of their employment belongs to the employer (unless otherwise agreed). This is the default rule — it does not need to be contractually stated, but stating it removes any ambiguity. Works created outside employment duties (on personal time, without company resources, unconnected to the role) may not be covered.
- **Patents Act 1977 s.39 [REQUIRED]:** employee inventions belong to the employer if made in the course of normal duties or specially assigned duties, or if the employee had a special obligation to further the employer's interests. The Act also sets out employee entitlement to compensation for unusually profitable inventions — rare in practice but real.
- **Trade Secrets (Enforcement, etc.) Regulations 2018 [REQUIRED]:** implements the EU Trade Secrets Directive into UK law (retained post-Brexit). Provides a legal framework for injunctions and damages for misappropriation of trade secrets. A "trade secret" has a specific definition — it must be kept confidential and have commercial value.
- **Common law duty of fidelity [REQUIRED — implied term]:** employees owe a duty of fidelity during employment — cannot compete, solicit colleagues or clients, or disclose confidential information. This is implied into every employment contract; the policy makes it explicit.
- **Post-termination: common law only [REQUIRED — no statute]:** post-termination restrictive covenants (non-competes, non-solicitation) are only enforceable if: (a) in writing in the contract, (b) protecting a legitimate business interest, and (c) no wider than reasonably necessary. Courts strike down overly broad covenants — "worldwide, 5-year non-compete" for a junior developer will be void.

### 3. Recommended sections

**What is confidential information** [REQUIRED]
> Define with examples: source code, product roadmap, unreleased features, user data, groomer data, pricing strategies, financial information, investor discussions, business development plans, technical architecture, security practices, API keys and credentials. Include a general catch-all (information the employee reasonably knows to be confidential) but do not rely on the catch-all alone — specificity makes enforcement much cleaner.

**What is NOT confidential** [REQUIRED — for clarity and to manage expectations]
> Information already in the public domain (not through breach of this policy); employees' own pre-existing knowledge and skills; general industry knowledge. Note: employees cannot be prevented from using general skills and experience gained in employment — this is the boundary between a confidentiality obligation and an unenforceable restraint.

**Obligations during employment** [REQUIRED]
> Handle confidential information securely; only access information needed for the role; do not disclose to third parties without authorisation; report any suspected breach immediately.

**Obligations after employment** [REQUIRED — must be in the contract to bind]
> Return all confidential information on termination (no copies retained on personal devices); continuing duty not to disclose; duration should be reasonable (typically 12–24 months, or in some cases indefinite for genuine trade secrets — get legal advice).

**IP ownership — the assignment** [REQUIRED]
> All works, inventions, discoveries, designs, and software created by the employee in the course of employment (or using company resources or confidential information) are assigned to and owned by the employer. Include a specific present-tense assignment ("the employee hereby assigns") — not just an obligation to assign in the future.

**Pre-existing IP** [REQUIRED — for employees who bring in IP]
> If an employee brings pre-existing IP to the role (e.g. open-source code they previously wrote, a tool they built independently), document this at onboarding. Without documentation, the company may inadvertently claim ownership of something that was not theirs to claim.

**Obligations to third parties** [REQUIRED — important for new hires]
> Employees must not use or disclose confidential information belonging to previous employers. They must confirm they are not bound by any restrictive covenant that would be breached by joining Groomr. If a previous employer covenant is discovered post-hire and is enforceable, the legal exposure falls on Groomr as well as the employee.

**Post-termination restrictions (restrictive covenants)** [COMMERCIAL CHOICE — legal input required]
> Non-solicitation of clients: reasonable, widely used, typically enforceable for 6–12 months.
> Non-solicitation of colleagues: reasonable, enforceable, typically 6–12 months.
> Non-compete: the most contentious. Enforceable only if narrow in scope, geography, and time. For a tech startup with a small team and concentrated competitive landscape, a narrow non-compete for senior roles (e.g. "not work directly for [named key competitors] for 6 months") is more defensible than a blanket clause. Get a solicitor to draft each covenant separately per role.

**Garden leave** [COMMERCIAL CHOICE — must be in contract]
> The ability to put a departing employee on garden leave during notice (paid, at home, away from work but bound by confidentiality duties) is a powerful tool to protect the business during a sensitive transition. Only available if the contract includes an express garden leave clause.

### 4. Marketplace-specific considerations
Code and platform architecture developed by employees is the core IP asset of Groomr. The platform's differential value (search algorithm, booking system, groomer matching, data model) depends on this IP being unambiguously owned by the company. Do not let first hires — especially engineers — start work without a signed employment contract including the IP assignment clause. "We'll sort the contract later" is how early-stage companies lose their IP.

### 5. Animal-welfare considerations
Not applicable.

### 6. Cross-references
- Use of Company Equipment Policy (secure return of devices containing confidential information)
- Social Media & Communications Policy (prohibition on disclosing confidential info on social platforms)
- Disciplinary & Grievance Policy (breach of confidentiality as gross misconduct)

### 7. Risk flags — get a solicitor on these
- **Every restrictive covenant must be tailored per role** — a blanket covenant in a template contract will almost certainly be challenged and may be struck down entirely, leaving you with no protection at all. Spend the time on this.
- **IP assignment must be in the contract, signed before work begins** — an assignment agreement signed after an employee has already written code may face a consideration argument (though under Scots law, consideration is not required — this matters less in Scotland than in E&W, but get the contract in place before day one regardless).
- **Open-source code:** if employees contribute to open-source projects that touch on company technology, you need a policy on this. Accidentally open-sourcing proprietary code is an irreversible event.

### 8. Decisions needed from you
- Non-compete: yes/no, and for which roles?
- Garden leave clause: yes/no (recommended for senior and technical roles)?
- Open-source contribution: permitted with approval, never, or unrestricted?
- Pre-existing IP documentation: will you use an IP disclosure schedule at onboarding?

## Policy 12: Performance Management Policy

### 1. Purpose & scope
- Governs how employee performance is assessed, how underperformance is identified and addressed, and the formal capability procedure that precedes any capability-based dismissal.
- Applies to all employees.
- The *positive* framework (objective setting, regular reviews, development support) is best practice. The formal capability procedure is legally required as the process that must precede any dismissal for performance reasons — without it, a dismissal is procedurally unfair.
- Distinct from the Disciplinary Procedure: disciplinary = misconduct; performance management = capability. Using the wrong procedure for the wrong problem is a common and costly error.

### 2. Regulatory drivers
- **Employment Rights Act 1996 [REQUIRED]:** capability (inability to perform the role to the required standard) is one of the five potentially fair reasons for dismissal. But the procedural fairness test still applies — the employer must follow a fair process.
- **ACAS Code of Practice on Disciplinary and Grievance Procedures 2015 [REQUIRED in effect]:** the ACAS Code explicitly covers capability as well as misconduct. An Employment Tribunal will consider whether the employer followed a reasonable capability process. Failure to do so = uplift on awards.
- **Equality Act 2010 [REQUIRED]:** underperformance caused by a disability must be addressed with reasonable adjustments before capability procedures are invoked. Dismissing a disabled employee for underperformance without first considering adjustments is disability discrimination.
- **Health and Safety at Work Act 1974 / Management Regs 1999 [BEST PRACTICE]:** poor performance caused by excessive workload, work-related stress, or inadequate training may be an employer-caused risk factor. The employer must rule this out before proceeding with a capability procedure.

### 3. Recommended sections

**Objective setting** [BEST PRACTICE]
> How and when performance objectives are set (at the start of the role, annually, or at the start of each quarter). SMART criteria or equivalent. Objectives should be documented and agreed — not unilaterally imposed. This is the foundation: a capability dismissal needs to show what standard was expected, that the employee knew it, and that they failed to meet it.

**Regular check-ins and one-to-ones** [BEST PRACTICE]
> Frequency (weekly/fortnightly for new hires; monthly for experienced employees). These are where issues are caught early and addressed informally. Document brief notes from each session — not verbatim minutes, but enough to show that conversations happened.

**Annual/half-year performance reviews** [BEST PRACTICE — commercially standard]
> Format, who conducts them, how ratings (if used) are calibrated, how development needs are identified, how the review links to pay decisions (if applicable).

**Identifying underperformance** [REQUIRED in process terms]
> What triggers a move from informal conversations to a formal process: persistent failure to meet defined objectives, quality of output below the agreed standard, pattern of missed deadlines. Be specific — "not meeting expectations" is too vague to build a case on.

**Informal stage — support and feedback** [REQUIRED before formal procedure]
> Before any formal warning: a clear conversation (documented) setting out the specific performance concern, the required standard, the timeframe for improvement, and the support available (training, coaching, revised objectives, check-ins). Many issues resolve here; if they don't, the documentation is the evidence.

**Formal stage — Performance Improvement Plan (PIP)** [REQUIRED]
> Written PIP: specific performance concerns, SMART improvement targets, support offered, timeframe (typically 4–12 weeks), review dates. The employee should be given the opportunity to comment on the PIP. A PIP is not a punishment — present it as a structured support plan. A tribunal that sees a PIP clearly drafted and communicated will give the employer much more credit than one that sees a sudden dismissal.

**Formal review hearing** [REQUIRED]
> At the end of the PIP period: a hearing to assess whether performance has improved. Right to be accompanied (ERA 1999). Outcome: satisfactory improvement (close the process), continued improvement required (extend or further PIP), or insufficient improvement (proceed to formal warning or, if this was a final stage, dismissal).

**Warnings and appeal** [REQUIRED — parallel to disciplinary process]
> Same structure as disciplinary warnings: written warning, final written warning, dismissal. Each decision appealable.

**Dismissal for capability** [REQUIRED]
> Only after: the employee knew the standard; was given a reasonable opportunity to meet it; received adequate support; and was warned that dismissal was a potential outcome. Written confirmation of dismissal with reasons. Notice (or PILON if contractual clause). Right of appeal.

**Health-related underperformance — the disability intersection** [REQUIRED]
> If underperformance is connected to a health condition (including mental health), pause the formal process and seek Occupational Health opinion. Consider reasonable adjustments. Do not proceed to a capability dismissal without confirming this route is exhausted — it is the most common source of successful discrimination claims against capability dismissals.

### 4. Marketplace-specific considerations
Not applicable — pure employment policy.

### 5. Animal-welfare considerations
Not applicable.

### 6. Cross-references
- Disciplinary & Grievance Policy (distinct procedure — same structural principles)
- Sickness Policy (health-related underperformance crossover)
- Equal Opportunities & Anti-Discrimination Policy (disability and reasonable adjustments)
- Personal Development Policy (development support offered during a PIP)

### 7. Risk flags — get a solicitor on these
- **Disability discrimination in capability dismissals** is the single highest-value risk in this policy area. Dismissal of an employee whose underperformance was caused or contributed to by a disability, without first considering adjustments, is automatically discriminatory and there is no cap on compensation for disability discrimination at Employment Tribunal.
- **Substantive vs. procedural fairness:** even a well-founded capability case can be lost on process. Get the warning letters and PIP format reviewed by a solicitor before first use — retrofitting template letters after a dismissal is too late.

### 8. Decisions needed from you
- Performance review cadence: annual, half-year, quarterly?
- Will you use a numerical rating system or a narrative-only review?
- Who hears formal capability hearings at this stage — the founder, or an external HR consultant?
- PIP duration: what is the standard window (4 weeks / 8 weeks / 12 weeks)?

## Policy 13: Redundancy Policy

### 1. Purpose & scope
- Governs the company's approach to redundancies: how the company identifies a redundancy situation, the selection and consultation process, entitlements, and the appeal process.
- Applies to all employees. Some statutory redundancy rights require qualifying service (2 years for statutory redundancy pay); the consultation and notice obligations apply regardless of service length.
- A redundancy is not a disciplinary matter — conflating the two (trying to "manage out" a poor performer via a redundancy process, or dismissing for conduct and calling it redundancy) is a legally dangerous route that leads to unfair dismissal and potentially wrongful dismissal claims simultaneously.

### 2. Regulatory drivers
- **Employment Rights Act 1996 Part XI [REQUIRED]:** definition of redundancy (the employer has ceased or intends to cease carrying on business, or has ceased or intends to cease carrying on business at the place where the employee is employed, or has diminished or expects to diminish the requirements for employees to carry out work of a particular kind). Statutory Redundancy Pay (SRP) for employees with 2+ years' service; calculated by reference to age, weekly pay, and length of service.
- **Trade Union and Labour Relations (Consolidation) Act 1992 [REQUIRED — triggered at 20+ redundancies]:** collective consultation rules kick in when 20 or more redundancies are proposed at one establishment within 90 days. Minimum consultation periods: 30 days for 20–99 proposed redundancies; 45 days for 100+. Notify the Secretary of State (HR1 form). At Groomr's current scale, collective consultation is unlikely to apply imminently, but document the trigger.
- **Employment Rights Act 1996 s.188A [REQUIRED]:** duty to individually consult in all redundancy situations, regardless of numbers.
- **TUPE Regulations 2006 [REQUIRED — if applicable]:** if the redundancy arises in the context of a business transfer or outsourcing, TUPE may apply (transferring employees to the new operator rather than making them redundant). The wrong call here is an automatic unfair dismissal. Flag the TUPE angle separately.
- **Equality Act 2010 [REQUIRED]:** selection criteria must not directly or indirectly disadvantage employees with protected characteristics. "Last in, first out" and attendance-based criteria frequently result in indirect discrimination claims (e.g. more women on maternity leave, more disabled employees with sickness absence).
- **Parental leave protections during redundancy [REQUIRED]:** employees on maternity, adoption, or shared parental leave have priority rights to be offered suitable alternative vacancies over other redundant employees (ERA 1996 s.47C and associated Regs). Failure to observe this is automatic unfair dismissal.

### 3. Recommended sections

**What constitutes a redundancy situation** [REQUIRED]
> Use the statutory definition (ERA 1996 s.139) explained in plain English. Key point: a genuine redundancy is about the *role* ceasing or reducing — not about the individual's performance or conduct. Document the business reason for the redundancy clearly before starting the process.

**Alternatives to redundancy** [REQUIRED — must be genuinely considered]
> Before commencing a formal redundancy process, consider: recruitment freeze, reduction in hours, overtime cuts, voluntary redundancy, redeployment. Document that these were considered. A tribunal that sees a redundancy where no alternatives were explored will be sceptical of whether it was genuine.

**Identifying the pool for selection** [REQUIRED]
> Define who is at risk (the pool) — this should include all employees doing substantially the same or similar work, not just the person the company wants to let go. Selecting the pool unfairly (engineering it to make a specific person redundant) is a classic unfair dismissal route.

**Selection criteria** [REQUIRED]
> Objective, measurable, and capable of being applied consistently. Common criteria: skills matrix/competency scores, attendance record, disciplinary record (live warnings only), performance (with documented evidence). Avoid criteria that disproportionately disadvantage protected groups: length of service (indirect age discrimination), attendance records (can disadvantage disabled employees or those on maternity leave). Score independently and document.

**Individual consultation** [REQUIRED]
> At-risk employees must be individually consulted: (1) first meeting — inform the employee of the at-risk status, the pool, the criteria, and the process; (2) provisional selection — inform the employee of their scores and proposed selection; allow them to respond, challenge scores, and suggest alternatives. Each meeting should be in writing (confirmed in writing after). Right to be accompanied at formal meetings.

**Collective consultation trigger** [REQUIRED — alert]
> If you reach 20 proposed redundancies at one establishment within 90 days, collective consultation rules apply — notify Insolvency Service (HR1 form within a specified period), establish/consult with employee representatives, observe the minimum 30-day consultation period before any dismissal takes effect.

**Suitable alternative employment** [REQUIRED]
> Before finalising redundancy, genuinely consider whether any suitable alternative role exists. Offer it to redundant employees. If an employee unreasonably refuses a suitable alternative offer, they lose entitlement to SRP. Document the offer and the employee's response.

**Notice** [REQUIRED]
> Redundancy dismissals require notice (contractual or statutory minimum, whichever is greater). Employees on notice have a right to reasonable time off to look for work (paid, subject to limits — ERA 1996 s.52). Payment in lieu of notice is possible if the PILON clause is in the contract.

**Statutory Redundancy Pay** [REQUIRED]
> 2+ years' service: calculated as 1.5 weeks' pay per year of service over 41, 1 week per year aged 22–40, 0.5 week per year under 22 (weekly pay capped at the statutory limit — verify current cap). SRP is not taxable up to the £30,000 threshold (alongside any other non-contractual termination payments).

**Enhanced redundancy pay (above statutory)** [COMMERCIAL CHOICE]
> Whether you pay above SRP. Common enhanced terms: no service cap, uncapped weekly pay, higher multipliers. If offered, document the basis — a practice of enhanced pay can become a contractual entitlement by custom and practice. Offer it explicitly rather than informally.

**Appeals** [REQUIRED]
> Right of appeal against selection, the redundancy decision, or the SRP calculation. Heard by someone not involved in the original decision. Outcome: uphold, vary, or reject.

**Outplacement support** [COMMERCIAL CHOICE]
> Offering career counselling, CV support, or reference letters is not required but is good practice and improves the dignity of the process.

### 4. Marketplace-specific considerations
Groomers on the platform are not employees — this policy does not apply to them. However, if Groomr ever reduces its platform offering significantly (e.g. ceases operations in a region), the communication to groomers is a platform/commercial matter governed by the Groomer Terms of Service, not this employment policy.

### 5. Animal-welfare considerations
Not applicable.

### 6. Cross-references
- Parental Leave Policy (priority vacancy rights for employees on family leave)
- Equal Opportunities & Anti-Discrimination Policy (non-discriminatory selection criteria)
- Disciplinary & Grievance Policy (redundancy is NOT a disciplinary matter — these must not be conflated)
- Employee Benefits Policy (any enhanced redundancy pay)

### 7. Risk flags — get a solicitor on these
- **"Pool of one" redundancies** (where the selection pool is engineered to contain only the person the company wants to remove) are a high tribunal risk. Selection pools must reflect genuine operational groupings.
- **Employees on family leave:** automatic unfair dismissal for failure to offer suitable alternative vacancies in priority. This is a zero-fault liability — the mistake alone constitutes the automatically unfair dismissal, regardless of intent.
- **TUPE:** if any element of a future restructure involves transferring a function to another business, an outsourcer, or a service-change, get TUPE advice before classifying it as redundancy. The consequences of a TUPE mis-call (liability transferring to the wrong party, ETO reasons, consultation failures) are complex and expensive.

- Will you offer enhanced redundancy pay above statutory? If yes, at what formula?
- Will you offer outplacement support?
- Who will manage the redundancy process at this stage — internal or external HR support?
