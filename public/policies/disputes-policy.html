1. About This Policy

This policy sets out how Groomr handles service disputes between dog owners and groomers. It explains what kinds of disputes Groomr will facilitate, how the process works, what each party can expect, and what the possible outcomes are.

It applies to both owners and groomers — either party can raise a dispute under this policy.

1.1 What this policy covers

This policy applies to disputes arising from the grooming service itself, including:

  • The quality of a groom being materially below what was agreed or advertised
  • A groomer failing to attend a confirmed appointment (groomer no-show)
  • An owner failing to bring their dog to a confirmed appointment (owner no-show)
  • Injury or distress to a dog during grooming
  • A groomer's refusal to perform a booked service without prior notice
  • Damage to owner property at groomer premises or during a mobile visit
  • Professional conduct concerns arising from a grooming appointment

1.2 What this policy does not cover

The following are outside the scope of this policy:

  • Payment chargebacks or Stripe-side disputes — these are handled between the relevant party, Stripe, and their card issuer
  • Deposit refunds following a cancellation — refer to Groomr's Refunds and Payments Policy
  • Tip disputes — tips are voluntary and are not subject to this process
  • Platform technical issues, account access problems, or billing for Groomr's own platform services

1.3 Relationship to other Groomr policies

This policy sits beneath Groomr's Terms of Service, which governs the platform as a whole. It is cross-referenced from the Groomer Terms of Engagement. Where a dispute outcome involves platform suspension or removal of a groomer, Groomr's Acceptable Use Policy also applies. Where a dispute involves a refund of a payment made through the platform, the Refunds and Payments Policy governs the mechanics of that refund.

2. Who Contracts With Whom

This is the most important section in this policy. Every question about who is liable for what flows from the relationship described here.

The grooming service is contracted directly between the dog owner and the independent groomer. When a booking is made and confirmed through Groomr, the owner and groomer enter a service contract with each other — not with Groomr.

Groomr is a platform. It facilitates discovery, booking, and payment between owners and groomers. Groomr does not employ groomers, does not direct how they carry out their work, and is not a party to the service contract formed when a booking is made.

This means that if something goes wrong with the grooming service itself — quality, conduct, injury — the owner's legal claim is against the groomer, not Groomr. Under the Consumer Rights Act 2015, the groomer is required to perform the service with reasonable care and skill. Groomr facilitates the dispute process to help both parties reach a resolution, but it is not the service provider and does not carry the groomer's liability.

Nothing in this policy or in Groomr's dispute process creates an employment relationship between Groomr and any groomer, or makes Groomr a party to any owner–groomer service contract.

3. What Qualifies as a Dispute

Groomr will facilitate a dispute where it relates to an appointment booked through the platform and falls within one of the following categories.

3.1 Service quality

The groom delivered was materially below the standard that was agreed at the time of booking, or materially below what was advertised in the groomer's listing. Minor differences from expectations are not disputes — the question is whether there was a material failure to deliver the booked service.

3.2 No-show by either party

Either the groomer failed to attend or open for a confirmed appointment, or the owner failed to bring their dog at the agreed time. Both are recognised dispute types — this process applies equally to both parties.

3.3 Injury or distress to a dog

A dog was injured, or suffered significant distress, during or as a direct result of the grooming service. Where injury is alleged, Groomr will request veterinary evidence where available. Please note that injury disputes may also engage animal welfare obligations — see section 11.

3.4 Refusal to perform a booked service

A groomer refused to carry out a confirmed booking, without having given prior notice of cancellation. Note: a groomer's decision to stop grooming a dog mid-appointment due to genuine welfare or safety concerns (such as aggression or a health issue) is not a dispute ground — see section 3.5 below.

3.5 Aggression and welfare-based refusal

Where a groomer refuses to proceed with or complete a groom because a dog displays aggression, cannot safely be handled, or has an undisclosed health condition that creates a risk, that refusal is not a valid dispute ground for the owner, provided the groomer took reasonable care in assessing and communicating the situation. Owners are reminded of their obligation to disclose relevant health and temperament information at the time of booking.

3.6 Property damage

Damage to owner property occurring at groomer premises or during a mobile visit, where the damage is alleged to have been caused during or in connection with the grooming appointment.

3.7 Professional conduct

Behaviour by either party during an appointment that falls below a reasonable standard of professional or civil conduct. Where conduct concerns relate to discrimination on the basis of a protected characteristic, Groomr's Acceptable Use Policy also applies.

4. Time Window for Raising a Dispute

A dispute must be raised through the Groomr platform within 14 days of the appointment date. This window is intended to give owners sufficient time for issues like skin reactions, coat damage, or post-groom health concerns to become apparent, while keeping the process useful for groomers who need to respond to fresh evidence.

The 14-day window governs Groomr's platform facilitation process only. It does not affect any right either party may have to pursue a legal remedy independently — see section 14 for governing law and your rights.

Your legal rights are not affected by this window. This 14-day limit applies to Groomr's facilitation process only. It does not extinguish any legal rights you may have under the Consumer Rights Act 2015, the Prescription and Limitation (Scotland) Act 1973, or any other applicable UK law. If you wish to pursue a claim through the courts or an ADR scheme independently of Groomr's process, you may do so regardless of whether the 14-day platform window has passed.

Where a dispute is raised after the 14-day window, Groomr may exercise discretion to facilitate it in exceptional circumstances — for example, where the delay was caused by a factor outside the raising party's control.

5. How to Raise a Dispute

5.1 Who can raise a dispute

Either the dog owner or the groomer can initiate a dispute. Both parties have equal standing in the process.

5.2 Where to raise a dispute

Disputes must be raised through the Groomr platform. Log in to your dashboard and navigate to the relevant appointment to begin the dispute process. If you are unable to access your account, contact disputes@groomr.uk for assistance.

5.3 What to include

When you raise a dispute, you will be asked to provide:

  • A clear description of the issue and what you believe went wrong
  • The appointment details (date, service booked, location)
  • Any supporting evidence — see section 5.4 below
  • The outcome you are seeking

Providing clear and specific information at the outset will help Groomr review your dispute promptly. Vague submissions may delay the process.

5.4 Evidence guidance

The following types of evidence are accepted:

  • Photographs of your dog before and after the groom
  • Photographs of any property damage
  • Veterinary notes or correspondence where injury is alleged
  • Screenshots of messages exchanged through the Groomr messaging system

We strongly encourage both parties to communicate through the Groomr platform wherever possible. Messages sent through the platform can be independently verified; off-platform communications (WhatsApp, text, email) cannot and will carry less evidential weight.

6. Groomr's Role in Disputes

Groomr is a facilitator, not a judge. The distinction matters for both parties — read this section carefully.

6.1 What Groomr will do

When a dispute is raised, Groomr will:

  • Acknowledge receipt and notify the other party
  • Provide a structured process for both parties to submit their account of events and any supporting evidence
  • Review all submissions and evidence provided
  • Propose a resolution where it considers it appropriate to do so
  • Notify both parties at each stage of the process

6.2 What Groomr will not do

Groomr is not an arbitrator, ombudsman, adjudicator, or court. This means:

  • Groomr's resolution proposals are not legally binding on either party
  • Groomr cannot compel a groomer to issue a refund, or compel an owner to accept a particular outcome
  • Groomr cannot award damages or make binding findings of fault
  • Groomr's facilitation of a dispute does not make Groomr a party to the owner–groomer service contract

The process is structured so that both parties propose and agree — Groomr facilitates and proposes, but acceptance is always a choice made by the parties themselves. This structure is deliberate and reflects Groomr's role as a neutral marketplace intermediary.

6.3 Intermediary status

As a platform intermediary under the Electronic Commerce (EC Directive) Regulations 2002, Groomr's involvement in the dispute process does not affect its status as a host. Groomr's role is to provide the structured dispute mechanism and to act on any notice of unlawful conduct or content — it does not adjudicate the quality of the service performed.

7. The Resolution Process

Groomr's dispute process has two rounds. The aim is to give both parties a fair opportunity to be heard and, wherever possible, to reach an agreed outcome without either party needing to take independent legal action.

7.1 Step 1 — Dispute raised and notified

The raising party submits the dispute through their Groomr dashboard. The other party is notified and asked to submit their own account of events. Groomr aims to acknowledge the dispute within 1 business day of it being raised.

7.2 Step 2 — Both parties submit their account

Both the owner and the groomer are asked to submit their written account of what happened, along with any supporting evidence. The process does not move to the next step until both submissions have been received (or until a reasonable deadline for submission has passed without response from one party).

7.3 Step 3 — Round 1 resolution proposal

Once both submissions have been received, Groomr reviews them and proposes a resolution. Groomr aims to put forward a Round 1 proposal within 5 business days of receiving both parties' submissions.

Both parties are then asked whether they accept the proposed resolution:

  • If both accept — the dispute is resolved and closed.
  • If either party rejects — the dispute moves to Round 2. The rejecting party will be asked to state their reason for rejecting the proposal.

7.4 Step 4 — Round 2 final resolution

Where a Round 1 proposal is rejected, Groomr reviews the rejection reasons and any additional information provided, and issues a final resolution. Both parties are asked to respond to the final resolution. Groomr aims to issue a final resolution within 10 business days of a Round 1 rejection being received.

After Round 2, Groomr closes the dispute. The platform process is concluded regardless of whether both parties accepted the final resolution.

Closure of the platform process does not prevent you from pursuing independent legal remedies. If you are unhappy with the outcome after Round 2, you remain free to pursue the matter through a certified ADR scheme or the courts — see sections 13 and 14.

7.5 Service level targets

The timeframes above are targets, not guarantees. Complex disputes, incomplete submissions, or periods of high volume may affect response times. Where Groomr anticipates a delay, it will notify both parties.

Summary of target timeframes:

  • Acknowledgement: within 1 business day of the dispute being raised
  • Round 1 resolution proposal: within 5 business days of both parties' submissions being received
  • Final resolution: within 10 business days of a Round 1 rejection being received

8. Evidence Handling

8.1 How evidence is reviewed

Evidence submitted as part of a dispute is reviewed by Groomr's admin team only. It is treated as confidential to the dispute.

8.2 Sharing between parties

Each party's written account and evidence will be summarised by Groomr before being shared with the other party. Groomr does not share raw submissions verbatim unless both parties consent. This approach is intended to allow both parties to understand the case against them fairly, while enabling Groomr to manage sensitive, distressing, or otherwise complex content appropriately.

8.3 Retention

Evidence submitted in connection with a dispute is personal data and will be handled in accordance with Groomr's Privacy Policy. Retention periods, your rights to access and erasure, and the lawful basis for processing are set out in that policy.

8.4 Prohibited content

Do not upload illegal content. Evidence submissions must not contain illegal material. Groomr is required by law to act on notified illegal content under the Online Safety Act 2023. Where submitted evidence contains or appears to contain illegal content, Groomr will take appropriate steps, which may include referral to the relevant authorities.

8.5 Animal welfare content

Where evidence submitted in a dispute indicates deliberate mistreatment of an animal, or conduct that may constitute a welfare offence under the Animal Health and Welfare (Scotland) Act 2006, the Animal Welfare Act 2006 (England and Wales), or the Welfare of Animals Act (Northern Ireland) 2011, Groomr will consider whether to refer the matter to the relevant animal welfare authority. In serious cases, Groomr will proactively refer — see section 11 for full details of the animal welfare pathway.

9. Possible Outcomes

9.1 What Groomr can recommend or do

Where Groomr proposes a resolution, the outcomes it can recommend or initiate include:

  • A partial or full refund of payment made through the platform (subject to the Refunds and Payments Policy)
  • A recommendation that the groomer offer a repeat groom or correction service at no charge
  • A recommendation that the groomer contact their public liability insurer (for injury or property damage claims)
  • A note on the groomer's internal platform record
  • A temporary suspension of the groomer's listing pending investigation (for serious welfare or safety allegations)
  • Closure of the dispute with no action, where the dispute is not substantiated

9.2 What Groomr cannot do

The following are outside Groomr's authority in this process:

  • Compelling a groomer to issue a refund directly to an owner outside of the platform payment system
  • Awarding compensation or damages to either party
  • Acting as a court, arbitrator, or ombudsman, or making legally binding determinations of fault
  • Guaranteeing any particular outcome, regardless of the evidence submitted
  • Acting as an insurer or handling insurance claims on behalf of either party

10. Refunds and Payments

Where a dispute resolution proposal includes a refund of payment made through the Groomr platform, the mechanics of that refund — including the process, timescales, and who bears the cost — are governed by Groomr's Refunds and Payments Policy, not this policy. The two policies must be read together where a refund is in play.

10.1 Groomr's payment facilitation role

Groomr facilitates payments between owners and groomers via Stripe. Groomr is not the merchant of record for the grooming service — it processes payment as an intermediary on behalf of the groomer. This affects how refunds are initiated and who bears the financial impact, which is set out in the Refunds and Payments Policy.

10.2 Overlap with Stripe chargebacks

Where an owner has already raised a dispute with their card issuer (a chargeback) in relation to the same appointment, Groomr will coordinate with the relevant parties but the outcome of the chargeback is determined by Stripe and the card issuer — it is not within Groomr's control and is separate from this dispute process. Running both processes simultaneously may affect the outcome of each.

11. Groomer Insurance and Animal Welfare

11.1 Insurance requirements

As a condition of listing on Groomr, groomers are required to hold valid public liability insurance that covers them for injury to animals in their care and for property damage. This requirement is set out in Groomr's Verification Policy and in the Groomer Terms of Engagement.

Where a dispute involves an injury or property damage claim and a groomer holds valid insurance, Groomr will provide the relevant appointment record and dispute documentation to assist the groomer in making an insurance claim, where requested and subject to the consent of both parties.

Groomr does not act as an insurer, does not handle insurance claims directly, and is not a party to any insurance contract between a groomer and their insurer.

11.2 Animal welfare during grooming

A groomer who is holding a dog for grooming has a duty of care to that animal under the applicable animal welfare legislation — the Animal Health and Welfare (Scotland) Act 2006 where the groom takes place in Scotland; the Animal Welfare Act 2006 where it takes place in England or Wales; and the Welfare of Animals Act (Northern Ireland) 2011 where it takes place in Northern Ireland. This duty exists independently of any dispute process.

Injury to a dog during grooming is not only a service-quality matter — depending on the circumstances, it may be a welfare matter. Where a dispute involves alleged injury, Groomr will request veterinary evidence where it is available.

11.3 Referral to animal welfare authorities

Where evidence submitted in a dispute indicates deliberate mistreatment of a dog, serious neglect, or conduct that may constitute a welfare offence, Groomr will proactively refer the matter to the relevant animal welfare authority:

  • Scotland: Scottish Society for the Prevention of Cruelty to Animals (SSPCA)
  • England and Wales: Royal Society for the Prevention of Cruelty to Animals (RSPCA)
  • Northern Ireland: Ulster Society for the Prevention of Cruelty to Animals (USPCA)

Referral is not a dispute outcome — it is a separate step Groomr may take as a platform operator where the welfare evidence warrants it. Referral to a welfare authority does not pre-empt the dispute process.

11.4 Common injury types and evidence

Coat damage, skin irritation, and clipper injuries are the most common injury-related disputes. For these claims, the most useful evidence is pre- and post-groom photographs and, where the condition is serious or persistent, a note from a veterinarian. Groomr will not guarantee any specific outcome on the basis of evidence submitted, but clear photographic evidence and professional veterinary assessment carry the most weight in the review process.

12. Impact on Groomer Listings

12.1 Public profile

Resolved or closed disputes are not shown on a groomer's public Groomr profile. Dispute history is not visible to owners searching the platform or viewing a groomer's listing.

12.2 Internal records

Groomr retains internal records of disputes associated with a groomer's account. A pattern of substantiated disputes — particularly disputes in the same category — may trigger an internal review of the groomer's standing on the platform. Groomr reserves the right to take steps under its Acceptable Use Policy where a pattern of behaviour warrants it.

12.3 Suspension during investigation

Where a dispute involves a serious allegation — in particular, an allegation of injury to a dog, deliberate mistreatment, or other conduct that poses a risk to animals or owners — Groomr may temporarily suspend the groomer's listing on the platform while the dispute is under investigation. Any such suspension is governed by Groomr's Acceptable Use Policy. A suspension does not constitute a finding of fault — it is a precautionary measure.

13. Alternative Dispute Resolution

If you have exhausted Groomr's dispute process and remain dissatisfied with the outcome, or if you wish to pursue the matter independently, you have the following options.

13.1 Certified ADR — Ombudsman Services

Groomr's nominated certified ADR provider is Ombudsman Services. Under the Alternative Dispute Resolution for Consumer Disputes Regulations 2015, where Groomr is unable to resolve a consumer complaint to the consumer's satisfaction, we are required to signpost this ADR route. Information about Ombudsman Services is available at ombudsman-services.org.

Use of the ADR scheme is voluntary for both parties. Groomr is not obliged to participate in an ADR scheme in every case, but will engage in good faith where the scheme operator requests it.

13.2 Court proceedings — Scotland

For lower-value claims (broadly up to £5,000) in Scotland, the Simple Procedure in the Sheriff Court provides a straightforward route for individuals to bring a claim without needing a solicitor. Information is available at scotcourts.gov.uk.

13.3 Court proceedings — England and Wales

For lower-value claims in England and Wales, the small claims track in the County Court (Money Claims Online) provides an accessible route. Information is available at gov.uk/make-court-claim-for-money.

13.4 Court proceedings — Northern Ireland

For lower-value claims in Northern Ireland, the Small Claims Court in the Magistrates' Court is the appropriate route. Information is available from the Northern Ireland Courts and Tribunals Service at justice-ni.gov.uk.

14. Governing Law and Jurisdiction

Groomr's dispute facilitation process, and this policy, are governed by Scots law. Unhinged Development Group Ltd is a Scottish-registered company, and the Scottish courts — the Sheriff Court and the Court of Session — have jurisdiction over claims brought against Groomr as a Scottish entity.

However, this clause does not override statutory consumer rights. If you are an owner or groomer based in England, Wales, or Northern Ireland, you retain the right to bring a claim against Groomr in the courts of your own jurisdiction, and to rely on the consumer protection laws applicable in your location. Groomr's choice of Scots law as the governing law for this policy cannot remove those rights.

As noted in section 4, Groomr's 14-day platform window for raising disputes does not extinguish any legal claim you may have. In Scotland, most contractual and civil claims are subject to the five-year short negative prescription under the Prescription and Limitation (Scotland) Act 1973. In England, Wales, and Northern Ireland, the Limitation Act 1980 sets a six-year limitation period for most civil claims. These statutory periods are not affected by this policy.
